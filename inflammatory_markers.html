Introduction: The Body's Alarm System

Inflammatory markers are the body's smoke detectors—they tell you something is burning, but not necessarily what or where. CRP rises rapidly with acute bacterial infections, ESR climbs slowly with chronic inflammatory conditions, and both can be elevated in everything from pneumonia to temporal arteritis. The skill lies not just in recognizing elevated values, but in understanding what type of inflammation you're seeing and how urgently you need to act.

This chapter transforms you from someone who simply flags abnormal inflammatory markers into a clinician who can distinguish acute from chronic inflammation, bacterial from viral infections, and serious from benign causes. You'll learn when to start antibiotics, when to investigate further, and when to simply monitor. Most importantly, you'll understand the NICE evidence-based approach to using CRP for antibiotic stewardship.

Clinical Pearl

"CRP is Fast, ESR is Slow": CRP rises within 4-6 hours of inflammation starting, peaks at 48 hours, and falls rapidly with treatment. ESR takes days to rise, peaks later, and falls slowly. Use CRP for acute decisions (bacterial vs viral, treatment response). Use ESR for chronic conditions (temporal arteritis, polymyalgia rheumatica, monitoring inflammatory diseases).

Inflammatory Markers Normal Ranges and Clinical Thresholds

Priority Assessment: When Inflammatory Markers Signal Emergency

Most elevated inflammatory markers represent manageable conditions requiring investigation or treatment, but certain patterns or levels demand immediate attention. These red flag scenarios can indicate life-threatening conditions requiring urgent hospital assessment or immediate antibiotic therapy.

CRP >200 mg/L with Fever
Severe bacterial infection, sepsis risk—immediate blood cultures, urgent antibiotics, consider hospital admission for IV therapy
ESR >100 mm/hr
Urgent investigation needed—consider malignancy, temporal arteritis, endocarditis, osteomyelitis. Same-day specialist referral if unwell
CRP >50 mg/L + Headache in >50 years
Temporal arteritis risk—immediate prednisolone 40-60mg, urgent ophthalmology referral, temporal artery biopsy within 7 days
Ferritin >1000 μg/L + Fever + Cytopenias
Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis possible—urgent hematology referral, consider bone marrow examination

Clinical Pearl

"The Giant Cell Arteritis Emergency": New headache with jaw claudication in anyone over 50 with elevated CRP/ESR is temporal arteritis until proven otherwise. Start prednisolone immediately—don't wait for biopsy. Irreversible blindness can occur within hours. The biopsy remains positive for up to 7 days after starting steroids, but vision loss is irreversible.

Step 1: CRP-Guided Antibiotic Decisions - NICE 2024 Approach

NICE guidelines have revolutionized respiratory infection management by providing evidence-based CRP thresholds for antibiotic prescribing. This approach reduces inappropriate antibiotic use while ensuring patients with bacterial infections receive prompt treatment. Understanding these thresholds and their limitations is essential for modern primary care.

Clinical Assessment First

Always assess clinical severity before CRP. Severe illness (high fever, rigors, severe dyspnea, hemodynamic instability) needs immediate antibiotics regardless of CRP level. CRP guides decisions in clinically uncertain cases.

Apply NICE CRP Thresholds

CRP <20 mg/L: Don't routinely offer antibiotics. CRP 20-100 mg/L: Consider delayed prescription. CRP >100 mg/L: Offer immediate antibiotic therapy. These thresholds reduce antibiotic prescribing by 20-30%.

Consider Point-of-Care Testing

Point-of-care CRP testing allows immediate decision-making in primary care. Results available within 3-5 minutes enable evidence-based prescribing decisions during the consultation.

Safety Net and Follow-up

Provide clear safety-netting advice regardless of CRP result. Delayed prescriptions for CRP 20-100 mg/L should have specific instructions for use if symptoms worsen or persist beyond 48-72 hours.

CRP <20 mg/L

Interpretation: Bacterial infection unlikely
Action: No routine antibiotics
Advice: Symptomatic treatment, safety-netting
Follow-up: If symptoms worsen or persist >7 days

CRP 20-100 mg/L

Interpretation: Possible bacterial infection
Action: Consider delayed antibiotic prescription
Instructions: Use if symptoms worsen after 48-72 hours
Benefit: Reduces antibiotic use by 40% vs immediate prescription

CRP >100 mg/L

Interpretation: Bacterial infection likely
Action: Immediate antibiotic therapy
Choice: First-line antibiotics as per local guidelines
Review: If no improvement in 48-72 hours

CRP >200 mg/L

Interpretation: Severe bacterial infection likely
Action: Urgent antibiotics ± hospital assessment
Investigations: Blood cultures, chest X-ray
Monitor: Clinical response, repeat CRP in 48-72 hours

NICE CRP-Guided Respiratory Infection Management

Patient with symptoms of lower respiratory tract infection
Clinical assessment: Severe illness present?
Yes (severe): Immediate antibiotics regardless of CRP
No (mild-moderate): Check CRP to guide antibiotic decision
CRP result guides: <20 no antibiotics, 20-100 delayed prescription, >100 immediate antibiotics

Clinical Pearl

"The 48-Hour CRP Rule": CRP peaks at 48 hours in bacterial infections. If treating based on high CRP (>100), expect CRP to fall by 50% within 48-72 hours with effective antibiotic therapy. If CRP isn't falling or continues rising, consider treatment failure, complications, or alternative diagnoses. This monitoring approach prevents both over-treatment and under-treatment.

Step 2: ESR - The Chronic Inflammation Detective

Normal ESR (Age-Adjusted)

General rule: Upper limit = (age + 10)/2 for women, age/2 for men
Young adults: <15 mm/hr (men), <20 mm/hr (women)
Elderly: Can be 40-50 mm/hr normally
Clinical: Chronic inflammatory disease unlikely

Mildly Elevated ESR (20-50 mm/hr)

Causes: Mild infection, early inflammatory disease
Age factor: May be normal in elderly patients
Investigation: Clinical correlation, consider repeat in 2-4 weeks
Action: Targeted investigation based on symptoms

Moderately Elevated ESR (50-100 mm/hr)

Causes: Active inflammatory disease, malignancy, infection
Investigation: FBC, biochemistry, autoimmune screen
Urgency: Usually requires systematic investigation
Conditions: RA, IBD, vasculitis, temporal arteritis

Highly Elevated ESR (>100 mm/hr)

Urgent causes: Temporal arteritis, endocarditis, malignancy
Investigation: Same-day assessment if unwell
Red flags: Headache >50 years, heart murmur, weight loss
Action: Urgent specialist referral often required

Conditions with Very High ESR (>100)

Vasculitis: Temporal arteritis, polymyalgia rheumatica

Malignancy: Multiple myeloma, lymphoma, solid tumors

Infection: Osteomyelitis, endocarditis, abscess

Inflammatory: SLE flare, severe RA

Factors Affecting ESR

Falsely low: Heart failure, sickle cell disease, polycythemia

Falsely high: Anemia, pregnancy, elderly age

Medications: Steroids lower ESR, heparin can elevate

Alternative: Plasma viscosity less affected by anemia

ESR vs CRP Comparison

ESR advantages: Better for chronic conditions, GCA screening

CRP advantages: Rapid response, specific for acute inflammation

Combined use: ESR high + CRP normal suggests chronic inflammation

Discordance: Investigate if results don't match clinical picture

Temporal Arteritis Red Flags

Symptoms: New headache, jaw claudication, visual symptoms

Age: Usually >50 years, peak incidence 70-80

ESR: Usually >50, often >100 mm/hr

Action: Immediate steroids, urgent biopsy

Clinical Pearl

"The ESR Age Rule": ESR normally increases with age—a rough guide is upper limit = age/2 for men, (age+10)/2 for women. An ESR of 40 might be normal at 80 but definitely abnormal at 20. Always interpret ESR in the context of patient age, and don't over-investigate elderly patients with mildly elevated ESR if they're clinically well.

Step 3: High-Sensitivity CRP - Cardiovascular Risk Assessment

High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) measures the same protein as regular CRP but can detect much lower levels. While regular CRP detects acute inflammation, hs-CRP reveals low-grade chronic inflammation associated with atherosclerosis and cardiovascular risk. This distinction is crucial for appropriate clinical use.

Understanding hs-CRP for CV Risk

hs-CRP provides additional risk stratification beyond traditional cardiovascular risk factors:

  • Low risk: <1.0 mg/L
  • Moderate risk: 1.0-3.0 mg/L
  • High risk: >3.0 mg/L
  • Acute inflammation: >10 mg/L (not reliable for CV risk)

hs-CRP adds prognostic information to traditional risk scores and may guide statin therapy decisions.

When to Use hs-CRP

hs-CRP is most useful in intermediate cardiovascular risk patients:

  • QRISK 10-20%: hs-CRP may refine risk assessment
  • Family history: Strong family history with borderline traditional risk
  • Statin decisions: When uncertain about starting statin therapy
  • Not useful: High or low traditional risk patients

hs-CRP <1.0 mg/L

CV Risk: Low additional risk
Action: Continue standard risk factor management
Statin decision: Based on traditional risk factors
Lifestyle: Maintain healthy lifestyle

hs-CRP 1.0-3.0 mg/L

CV Risk: Moderate additional risk
Action: Enhanced lifestyle intervention
Consider: Statin therapy if borderline traditional risk
Monitoring: Annual assessment of risk factors

hs-CRP >3.0 mg/L

CV Risk: High additional risk
Action: Intensive risk factor management
Statin therapy: Stronger consideration
Investigation: Exclude other inflammatory conditions

hs-CRP >10 mg/L

Interpretation: Acute inflammation likely
CV Risk: Cannot reliably assess until inflammation resolves
Action: Investigate and treat acute inflammation first
Repeat: Recheck hs-CRP after resolution

Clinical Pearl

"The hs-CRP Sweet Spot": hs-CRP is most useful in patients with intermediate cardiovascular risk (QRISK 10-20%) where you're uncertain about statin therapy. It adds little value in obviously high-risk patients (who need statins anyway) or very low-risk patients (who don't need additional risk stratification). Use it to refine borderline decisions, not as a general screening test.

Step 4: Procalcitonin - The Bacterial Infection Marker

Understanding Procalcitonin Physiology

Procalcitonin is released specifically in response to bacterial infections and sepsis. Viral infections, autoimmune conditions, and localized infections cause minimal PCT elevation, making it more specific for bacterial infection than CRP.

Procalcitonin Interpretation Thresholds

PCT <0.25 ng/mL: Bacterial infection unlikely. PCT 0.25-0.5 ng/mL: Possible bacterial infection. PCT 0.5-2.0 ng/mL: Probable bacterial infection. PCT >2.0 ng/mL: Severe bacterial infection/sepsis likely.

Clinical Applications in Primary Care

PCT is most useful for distinguishing bacterial from viral respiratory infections, guiding antibiotic decisions in pneumonia, and monitoring treatment response in hospitalized patients with bacterial infections.

Limitations and Considerations

PCT can be elevated in severe trauma, burns, surgery, and some autoimmune conditions. It's less useful in immunocompromised patients. Cost and availability limit routine use in primary care compared to CRP.

PCT <0.25 ng/mL

Interpretation: Bacterial infection very unlikely
Action: Consider withholding antibiotics
Viral infection: Most likely cause if symptomatic
Safety: Excellent negative predictive value

PCT 0.25-0.5 ng/mL

Interpretation: Low probability bacterial infection
Clinical correlation: Consider severity of illness
Action: Often appropriate to avoid antibiotics
Follow-up: Clinical monitoring essential

PCT 0.5-2.0 ng/mL

Interpretation: Probable bacterial infection
Action: Antibiotic therapy usually indicated
Severity: Moderate bacterial infection likely
Monitoring: Expect PCT to fall with effective treatment

PCT >2.0 ng/mL

Interpretation: Severe bacterial infection/sepsis
Action: Immediate antibiotic therapy
Urgency: Consider hospital assessment
Prognosis: Higher levels associated with worse outcomes

Clinical Pearl

"PCT's Viral-Bacterial Discrimination": PCT's greatest strength is distinguishing bacterial from viral infections. In viral respiratory infections, PCT typically stays <0.25 ng/mL even when patients are quite unwell. In bacterial pneumonia, PCT usually rises >0.5 ng/mL. This makes PCT particularly useful when CRP is equivocal (20-100 mg/L) and you need to decide about antibiotics.

Step 5: Putting It All Together - Inflammatory Marker Strategy

Acute Infection Assessment

First choice: CRP for rapid decision-making

Thresholds: <20 no antibiotics, >100 give antibiotics

PCT addition: If bacterial vs viral uncertain

ESR role: Limited in acute infections

Chronic Inflammation Investigation

First choice: ESR for screening chronic conditions

Age-adjusted: Remember normal ESR increases with age

CRP addition: To assess current disease activity

Investigation: ESR >50 usually needs explanation

Treatment Monitoring

CRP: Rapid response to effective antibiotic therapy

ESR: Slower response, useful for chronic conditions

Frequency: CRP at 48-72 hours, ESR weekly

Targets: 50% reduction suggests good response

Cardiovascular Risk

hs-CRP: Only useful in intermediate-risk patients

Timing: Avoid during acute illness or inflammation

Integration: Combine with traditional risk factors

Action: May influence statin therapy decisions

Inflammatory Marker Selection Guide

Patient with suspected inflammatory condition
Acute symptoms (<2 weeks) or chronic symptoms (>2 weeks)?
Acute: CRP first choice, consider PCT if bacterial vs viral unclear
Chronic: ESR first choice, add CRP to assess current activity
CV Risk: hs-CRP only in intermediate-risk patients when well

Common Clinical Scenarios and Marker Choice

  • Respiratory infection: CRP using NICE thresholds (<20, 20-100, >100)
  • New headache >50 years: ESR ± CRP to screen for temporal arteritis
  • Joint pain and stiffness: ESR to screen for inflammatory arthritis
  • Unexplained weight loss: ESR ± CRP to screen for malignancy
  • Monitoring treated infection: CRP to assess treatment response
  • Monitoring RA/IBD: CRP or ESR to assess disease activity

Clinical Pearl

"The 48-Hour Inflammatory Response Rule": Effective antibiotic therapy should cause CRP to start falling within 48 hours and ESR within a week. If inflammatory markers continue rising despite appropriate antibiotics, consider treatment failure, complications (abscess), resistant organisms, or alternative diagnoses. This monitoring approach prevents prolonged ineffective therapy.

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

The ESR Age Ignorance

Mistake: Using same ESR reference ranges for all ages
Reality: ESR normally increases with age
Solution: Use age-adjusted upper limits
Guide: Upper limit ≈ age/2 (men), (age+10)/2 (women)

The CRP Viral Confusion

Mistake: Giving antibiotics for viral infections with mildly raised CRP
Reality: Viral infections can cause CRP 20-50 mg/L
Solution: Use NICE thresholds—<20 no antibiotics, consider PCT
Clinical: Viral symptoms usually obvious

The hs-CRP Overuse

Mistake: Using hs-CRP as general screening test
Reality: Only useful in intermediate CV risk patients
Solution: Reserve for QRISK 10-20% when uncertain about statins
Avoid: Testing during acute illness (unreliable for CV risk)

The Single Marker Tunnel Vision

Mistake: Relying on inflammatory markers without clinical context
Reality: Markers guide decisions but don't make diagnoses
Solution: Always integrate with history, examination, other tests
Remember: Treat the patient, not the number

Red Flags in Inflammatory Marker Interpretation

  • Very high markers with mild symptoms: Consider serious underlying disease
  • Normal markers with severe illness: Doesn't exclude serious infection (immunocompromised)
  • Rising markers on treatment: Treatment failure, complications, or wrong diagnosis
  • Persistently high markers: Need systematic investigation for underlying cause
  • Discordant clinical picture: Re-evaluate diagnosis and consider other tests

Key Inflammatory Marker Thresholds

CRP antibiotic guidance: <20 no antibiotics, 20-100 consider delayed, >100 immediate

ESR urgent investigation: >100 mm/hr needs same-day assessment

hs-CRP CV risk: <1 low, 1-3 moderate, >3 high risk

PCT bacterial infection: <0.25 unlikely, >0.5 probable bacterial

Clinical Decision Points

Emergency action: CRP >200 with fever, ESR >100 with symptoms

Temporal arteritis: ESR >50 + headache >50 years = urgent steroids

Treatment monitoring: 50% reduction expected in 48-72 hours (CRP)

Investigation threshold: Unexplained persistent elevation needs workup

Clinical Pearl

"The Inflammatory Marker Triangle": Think of CRP, ESR, and clinical assessment as three corners of a triangle—you need at least two to make good decisions. High CRP with compatible clinical picture supports bacterial infection. Normal markers with severe illness suggests viral infection or non-inflammatory cause. Let the clinical picture guide which markers to order and how to interpret them.

Take Home Messages for Inflammatory Markers

  • Use NICE CRP thresholds: Evidence-based antibiotic prescribing reduces resistance
  • Choose markers wisely: CRP for acute, ESR for chronic conditions
  • Age-adjust ESR: Normal values increase with age
  • Monitor treatment response: Markers should fall with effective therapy
  • Clinical context crucial: Markers guide decisions but don't make diagnoses
  • Know emergency thresholds: Very high values need urgent assessment
