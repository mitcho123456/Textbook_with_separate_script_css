Introduction: The Thyroid's Metabolic Orchestra

Thyroid function tests are like listening to a metabolic orchestra where TSH conducts from the pituitary podium, free T4 plays the steady baseline melody, and T3 provides the high-energy flourishes. When this orchestra plays in harmony, metabolism hums along perfectly. When it goes awry, patients experience the full spectrum from the sluggish exhaustion of hypothyroidism to the racing anxiety of hyperthyroidism.

This chapter transforms you from someone who simply orders "thyroid function" into a clinician who understands the elegant feedback loops, recognizes subtle patterns of dysfunction, and knows when borderline results matter and when they don't. You'll learn to distinguish primary thyroid disease from secondary disorders, understand when subclinical disease needs treatment, and avoid the common pitfalls that lead to unnecessary anxiety and overtreatment.

Clinical Pearl

"TSH is the Sensitive Early Warning": TSH changes before T4 and T3 become obviously abnormal. A TSH of 8 mIU/L with normal T4 (subclinical hypothyroidism) represents earlier disease than a TSH of 8 with low T4 (overt hypothyroidism). TSH amplifies small changes in thyroid hormone levels, making it your most sensitive screening test.

Priority Assessment: When Thyroid Tests Signal Emergency

Most thyroid dysfunction develops slowly and can be managed in primary care. However, certain patterns represent medical emergencies requiring immediate hospital assessmentâ€”particularly thyroid storm in hyperthyroidism or myxedema coma in severe hypothyroidism. Recognition of these patterns and knowing when to act urgently versus when to investigate gradually can be life-saving.

Clinical Pearl

"The Subclinical Atrial Fibrillation Risk": Even subclinical hyperthyroidism (low TSH, normal T4/T3) increases atrial fibrillation risk by 60%. Any patient with new AF should have thyroid function checked urgently. Treating the hyperthyroidism often restores normal sinus rhythm and reduces stroke risk.

Step 1: The TSH-First Strategy - NICE 2019 Approach

NICE NG145 revolutionized thyroid testing by establishing TSH as the single best screening test for thyroid dysfunction. This evidence-based approach reduces unnecessary testing, focuses on clinically relevant abnormalities, and provides a systematic framework for interpretation that works in 95% of cases.

Start with TSH Alone

TSH is the most sensitive initial test for thyroid dysfunction. It detects both hyper- and hypothyroidism before T4/T3 levels become obviously abnormal. Only order additional tests if TSH is outside normal range.

Use Reflex Testing Strategy

If TSH abnormal, labs automatically add free T4. If TSH very low (<0.1), may also add T3. This cascading approach ensures appropriate testing without overordering unnecessary tests.

Interpret the Pattern

High TSH = hypothyroidism (primary thyroid failure). Low TSH = hyperthyroidism (or over-treatment). Normal TSH with symptoms = usually not thyroid disease, consider other causes.

Consider Clinical Context

Pregnancy, medications (amiodarone, lithium), acute illness, and central thyroid disease can all affect interpretation. Always correlate biochemistry with clinical presentation.

Step 2: Hypothyroidism - The Metabolic Slowdown

Subclinical Hypothyroidism

Pattern: TSH 4-10 mIU/L, normal free T4
Prevalence: 5-10% of adults, more common in women
Symptoms: Often subtle or absent
Treatment: Consider if symptomatic, pregnant, or high cardiovascular risk

Overt Hypothyroidism

Pattern: TSH >10 mIU/L, low free T4
Symptoms: Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation
Treatment: Levothyroxine replacement indicated
Monitoring: TSH every 6-8 weeks until stable

Severe Hypothyroidism

Pattern: TSH >20 mIU/L, very low T4 <5 pmol/L
Symptoms: Severe fatigue, bradycardia, hypothermia
Complications: Heart failure, effusions, coma risk
Treatment: Urgent levothyroxine, consider hospital assessment

Central Hypothyroidism

Pattern: Low/normal TSH with low T4
Cause: Pituitary or hypothalamic disease
Clues: Other pituitary hormone deficiencies
Action: Urgent endocrinology referral, MRI pituitary

Hypothyroidism Treatment Initiation Guidelines

TSH 4-6.9 mIU/L

Treatment if: Symptomatic + positive TPO antibodies
Or if: Pregnancy, trying to conceive
Monitor: TSH annually if not treating
Dose: 25-50 mcg levothyroxine daily

TSH 7-9.9 mIU/L

Treatment: Usually recommended
Especially if: Symptoms, cardiovascular disease
Start: 50 mcg levothyroxine (25 mcg if elderly/cardiac)
Monitor: TSH at 6-8 weeks

TSH â‰¥10 mIU/L

Treatment: Always recommended (NICE threshold)
Start: 50-100 mcg levothyroxine
Urgent if: Severe symptoms, cardiac complications
Target TSH: 0.4-2.5 mIU/L

TSH >20 mIU/L

Treatment: Urgent levothyroxine replacement
Dose: 100-150 mcg daily (if no cardiac disease)
Monitor: Weekly initially, watch for cardiac effects
Complications: Heart failure, myxedema coma risk

Hypothyroidism Risk Factors

Demographics: Female, age >60, family history

Autoimmune: Type 1 diabetes, celiac disease, vitiligo

Medications: Amiodarone, lithium, interferon

Previous: Thyroid surgery, radioiodine, neck irradiation

Hashimoto's Disease Features

Antibodies: Positive TPO antibodies (90% cases)

Goiter: Often present, may be nodular

Progression: Gradual onset over months to years

Associated: Other autoimmune conditions common

Treatment Monitoring

Initial: TSH every 6-8 weeks after dose changes

Stable: Annual TSH once optimal dose established

Target: TSH 0.4-2.5 mIU/L for most patients

Pregnancy: TSH <2.5 mIU/L in first trimester

Special Situations

Elderly: Start 25 mcg, titrate slowly

Cardiac disease: Start 25 mcg, monitor ECG

Pregnancy: Increase dose by 25-50%, urgent monitoring

Malabsorption: May need higher doses or liquid preparation

Clinical Pearl

"The Levothyroxine Timing Rule": Levothyroxine absorption is reduced by 40-50% if taken with food, coffee, or calcium. Always advise taking on an empty stomach, 30-60 minutes before breakfast, with water only. This simple instruction prevents most treatment failures and reduces the need for dose escalation.

Step 3: Hyperthyroidism - The Metabolic Acceleration

Understanding Hyperthyroidism Causes

Hyperthyroidism isn't one diseaseâ€”it's a syndrome with multiple causes requiring different treatments:

  • Graves' disease (70%): Autoimmune, TSH receptor antibodies positive
  • Toxic multinodular goiter (20%): Usually older patients, nodular thyroid
  • Toxic adenoma (5%): Single hot nodule on scan
  • Thyroiditis (5%): Transient, often post-viral or post-partum

Distinguishing True Hyperthyroidism

Not all low TSH represents hyperthyroidism needing treatment:

  • Subclinical hyperthyroidism: Low TSH, normal T4/T3
  • Non-thyroidal illness: Sick euthyroid syndrome
  • Medication effects: Over-replacement with levothyroxine
  • Pregnancy: hCG-mediated TSH suppression (normal)

Graves' Disease Pattern

Biochemistry: TSH <0.1, elevated T4 and T3
Antibodies: TRAb positive (>95% cases)
Clinical: Diffuse goiter, eye signs, pretibial myxedema
Treatment: Antithyroid drugs, radioiodine, or surgery

Toxic Multinodular Goiter

Biochemistry: TSH <0.1, T4/T3 elevated
Clinical: Nodular goiter, usually older patients
Scan: Multiple hot areas
Treatment: Radioiodine preferred, surgery if large

Thyroiditis Pattern

Phase 1: TSH low, T4/T3 high (hyperthyroid)
Phase 2: TSH high, T4/T3 low (hypothyroid)
Phase 3: Recovery to normal (usually)
Treatment: Beta-blockers, avoid antithyroid drugs

Subclinical Hyperthyroidism

Biochemistry: TSH <0.4, normal T4/T3
Symptoms: Often minimal or absent
Risks: Atrial fibrillation, osteoporosis
Treatment: Consider if >65 years or cardiovascular disease

Confirm Hyperthyroidism

Low TSH with elevated T4 and/or T3 confirms hyperthyroidism. If T4/T3 normal, consider subclinical hyperthyroidism or non-thyroidal illness.

Determine the Cause

Check TRAb to diagnose Graves' disease. If negative, consider thyroid scan to distinguish toxic nodular disease from thyroiditis.

Assess Severity and Complications

Check for atrial fibrillation (ECG), heart failure, and thyroid eye disease. Severe hyperthyroidism needs urgent treatment.

Initiate Appropriate Treatment

Beta-blockers for symptom control, antithyroid drugs for Graves' disease, radioiodine for toxic nodular disease. Refer to endocrinology.

Hyperthyroidism Treatment Thresholds

  • TSH <0.1 + symptomatic: Treat regardless of T4/T3 levels
  • TSH <0.1 + asymptomatic + >65 years: Consider treatment (AF risk)
  • T4 >40 pmol/L or T3 >10 pmol/L: Urgent treatment needed
  • New atrial fibrillation: Immediate anticoagulation and rate control
  • Thyroid storm risk: Fever, tachycardia >130, altered mental state
  • Pregnancy with hyperthyroidism: Urgent obstetric-endocrine referral

Clinical Pearl

"The T3 Toxicosis Clue": Some patients have isolated T3 elevation with normal T4â€”this is genuine hyperthyroidism requiring treatment. Always check T3 when TSH is suppressed, especially if T4 is normal but patient has hyperthyroid symptoms. T3 toxicosis is more common in iodine-deficient areas and nodular thyroid disease.

Step 4: Special Situations and Tricky Patterns

Pregnancy Thyroid Changes

Normal changes: TSH falls in first trimester due to hCG
Reference ranges: Different for each trimester
Hypothyroidism: TSH >2.5 in first trimester
Hyperthyroidism: Distinguished from hyperemesis gravidarum

Non-Thyroidal Illness

Pattern: Low T3, low/normal T4, variable TSH
Cause: Severe acute illness, starvation
Recovery: Usually normalizes as illness resolves
Treatment: Generally not needed, treat underlying illness

Amiodarone Effects

Type 1: Hyperthyroidism in nodular goiter
Type 2: Destructive thyroiditis
Monitoring: TFTs every 6 months on amiodarone
Management: Complex, needs specialist input

Central Thyroid Disease

Pattern: Low T4 with low/normal TSH
Cause: Pituitary or hypothalamic disease
Clues: Other pituitary hormone deficiencies
Action: Urgent endocrinology referral, pituitary MRI

Drug-Induced Thyroid Dysfunction

Hypothyroidism: Amiodarone, lithium, interferon

Hyperthyroidism: Amiodarone, iodine contrast, levothyroxine

Monitoring: Baseline and regular TFTs on these drugs

Action: Discuss with prescriber before stopping

Thyroid Disease in Elderly

Presentation: Often atypical or subtle symptoms

Hyperthyroidism: May present as depression, AF, heart failure

Treatment: Start lower doses, monitor carefully

Targets: May accept slightly higher TSH (up to 6 mIU/L)

Subclinical Disease Decisions

Subclinical hypothyroidism: Treat if TSH >10, symptoms, or pregnancy

Subclinical hyperthyroidism: Treat if >65, cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis

Monitoring: 6-monthly if not treating

Antibodies: TPO positive increases progression risk

Thyroid Antibody Interpretation

TPO antibodies: Hashimoto's disease (90% sensitivity)

TRAb: Graves' disease (95% specificity)

Thyroglobulin antibodies: Less specific, often with TPO

Clinical use: Confirm autoimmune cause, predict progression

Clinical Pearl

"The Sick Euthyroid Trap": Severely ill patients often have low T3, low/normal T4, and variable TSH (sick euthyroid syndrome). This is an adaptive response, not thyroid disease. Don't start thyroid hormone replacement in critically ill patients based on these resultsâ€”treat the underlying illness and recheck TFTs after recovery.

Step 5: Monitoring and Follow-up Strategies

Thyroid Function Monitoring Timeline

Hypothyroidism Treatment Monitoring

TSH at 6-8 weeks after starting or changing levothyroxine dose. Continue adjusting until TSH 0.4-2.5 mIU/L. Then annual monitoring once stable dose achieved.

Hyperthyroidism Treatment Monitoring

TSH, FT4, FT3 every 6 weeks during antithyroid drug treatment. Once in remission, TSH every 3 months. Watch for relapse after stopping treatment.

Subclinical Disease Monitoring

If not treating subclinical hypothyroidism: TSH annually (or every 2-3 years if no symptoms). If subclinical hyperthyroidism: TSH every 6 months, watch for progression.

High-Risk Patient Monitoring

Pregnancy: TSH each trimester. Amiodarone: TSH every 6 months. Post-radioiodine: TSH at 6-8 weeks, then 6-monthly lifelong (hypothyroidism risk).

When to Refer to Endocrinology

  • New hyperthyroidism: All cases need specialist assessment for cause and treatment
  • Thyroid eye disease: Urgent referral if sight-threatening features
  • Pregnancy with thyroid disease: Joint obstetric-endocrine care needed
  • Difficult-to-treat cases: Poor response to standard treatment
  • Central thyroid disease: Low T4 with low/normal TSH
  • Complex drug interactions: Amiodarone-induced thyroid dysfunction

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

The Normal TSH Overconfidence

Mistake: Assuming normal TSH excludes all thyroid disease
Reality: Central thyroid disease has low T4 with normal/low TSH
Solution: Consider T4 if pituitary disease suspected
Clues: Other pituitary hormone deficiencies

The Subclinical Overtreatment

Mistake: Treating all TSH 4-10 mIU/L with normal T4
Reality: Many are asymptomatic and don't benefit
Solution: Follow NICE threshold of TSH â‰¥10 mIU/L
Consider treatment: If symptomatic, pregnant, or high CV risk

The Pregnancy TSH Panic

Mistake: Using non-pregnant reference ranges in pregnancy
Reality: TSH naturally falls in first trimester due to hCG
Solution: Use trimester-specific reference ranges
Target: TSH <2.5 mIU/L in first trimester

The Sick Patient Thyroid Testing

Mistake: Ordering TFTs in acutely unwell patients
Reality: Sick euthyroid syndrome gives misleading results
Solution: Avoid TFTs in acute illness unless thyroid storm suspected
Timing: Recheck 6-8 weeks after recovery

Red Flags in Thyroid Function Testing

  • TSH and T4 moving in same direction: Consider central thyroid disease, non-thyroidal illness
  • Normal TSH with obvious hypo/hyperthyroid symptoms: Recheck timing, consider other diagnoses
  • Extreme TSH values (>20 or <0.01): Need urgent clinical assessment and treatment
  • Discordant biochemistry and clinical picture: Consider drug effects, recent illness
  • Poor treatment response: Check compliance, absorption, drug interactions

"The Thyroid's Slow Dance": Thyroid function changes slowlyâ€”allow 6-8 weeks between dose adjustments for full effect. TSH has a long half-life, so early retesting gives false reassurance.
