Introduction: The Deficiency Detective Work

Iron deficiency, B12 deficiency, and folate deficiency are among the most common and treatable causes of anaemia worldwide. Yet diagnosis can be complex, with overlapping presentations and confounding factors. Modern testing allows precise diagnosis, but requires systematic interpretation. Understanding when to test, how to interpret complex patterns, and crucially how to investigate underlying causes will transform your management of these common conditions.

Clinical Pearl

"Ferritin is Your Friend, But Not Always Honest": Ferritin <15 ¬µg/L always means iron deficiency. Ferritin >100 ¬µg/L usually excludes iron deficiency. But ferritin 15-100 ¬µg/L in the presence of inflammation can still represent iron deficiency. This is when you need the full iron studies panel.

Step 1: Iron Studies - Systematic Interpretation

Start with Ferritin - The Foundation Test

Ferritin <15 ¬µg/L definitively confirms iron deficiency. Ferritin >100 ¬µg/L makes iron deficiency very unlikely. Ferritin 15-100 ¬µg/L requires additional tests to clarify, especially if inflammation is present.

Add Transferrin Saturation for Clarity

TSAT <20% strongly suggests iron deficiency even with normal ferritin in inflammatory states. TSAT >50% may indicate iron overload. Most reliable single test in chronic disease.

Consider Inflammatory Markers

Raised CRP/ESR can elevate ferritin, masking iron deficiency. In inflammation, use lower ferritin thresholds (<45-100 ¬µg/L) combined with TSAT <20% for diagnosis.

Integrate with FBC Pattern

Microcytic anaemia (MCV <80 fL) with iron deficiency confirms iron deficiency anaemia. Normal MCV doesn't exclude early iron deficiency - iron stores deplete before red cell changes occur.

Clinical Pearl

"The AGA 45 Rule": American Gastroenterological Association recommends ferritin <45 ¬µg/L as the diagnostic threshold for iron deficiency in anaemia (85% sensitivity, 92% specificity). This higher threshold prevents missing iron deficiency in inflammatory conditions.

Iron Deficiency Pattern

Classic: ‚Üì Ferritin (<15), ‚Üì TSAT (<20%), ‚Üì Serum Iron, ‚Üë TIBC
With Inflammation: Ferritin 15-100, ‚Üì TSAT (<20%), ‚Üì Serum Iron
Early Stage: ‚Üì Ferritin, Normal Hb/MCV initially
Action: Investigate cause, start iron replacement

Anaemia of Chronic Disease

Classic: Normal/‚Üë Ferritin (>100), Normal/‚Üì TSAT (15-25%), ‚Üì Serum Iron, Normal TIBC
Clinical Context: Underlying inflammatory condition known
Challenge: Can coexist with true iron deficiency
Strategy: Consider therapeutic trial of iron if TSAT <20%

Iron Overload Pattern

Classic: ‚Üë Ferritin (>300), ‚Üë TSAT (>50%), ‚Üë Serum Iron, Normal/‚Üì TIBC
Causes: Hereditary haemochromatosis, repeated transfusions
Action: Check HFE gene mutations, assess for organ damage
Urgency: Early detection prevents irreversible complications

Mixed Pattern (Iron Deficiency + Chronic Disease)

Complex: Ferritin 45-100, TSAT <20%, ‚Üì Serum Iron
Context: Chronic disease with ongoing iron loss
Example: IBD, chronic kidney disease, heart failure
Management: Treat iron deficiency AND underlying condition

Step 2: B12 Deficiency - Beyond the Guidelines (NICE NG239 2024)

Neurological B12 Deficiency
Subacute combined degeneration, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive impairment - treat urgently even with normal B12 levels
B12 <150 ng/L with Symptoms
Severe deficiency requiring immediate treatment with 1000 ¬µg alternate days until improvement
Pancytopenia + Macrocytosis
Severe B12/folate deficiency can mimic leukaemia - urgent haematology discussion
Normal B12 + Strong Clinical Suspicion
Functional B12 deficiency possible - check MMA, homocysteine, or therapeutic trial

NICE NG239 2024 Key Updates

Terminology Change:

  • NICE now uses "autoimmune gastritis" instead of "pernicious anaemia"
  • True pernicious anaemia (life-threatening) now rare due to earlier detection
  • Focus shifted to underlying gastric pathology

Diagnostic Approach:

  • Don't rule out deficiency based on normal Hb or MCV alone
  • B12 deficiency can present with neuropsychiatric symptoms only
  • Consider functional deficiency if strong clinical suspicion

Testing Strategy:

  • Check anti-intrinsic factor antibodies if B12 low
  • Consider MMA/homocysteine if B12 borderline
  • Test for autoimmune gastritis markers

Treatment Protocols (NICE NG239)

With Neurological Symptoms:

  • Hydroxocobalamin 1000 ¬µg IM alternate days
  • Continue until no further improvement
  • Then 1000 ¬µg every 2 months lifelong

Without Neurological Symptoms:

  • Hydroxocobalamin 1000 ¬µg IM 3x weekly for 2 weeks
  • Then 1000 ¬µg every 2-3 months lifelong

Autoimmune Gastritis (Pernicious Anaemia):

  • Lifelong treatment required
  • Annual gastric cancer surveillance recommended
  • Monitor for other autoimmune conditions

Clinical Pearl

"Treat the Patient, Not the Number": NICE NG239 emphasizes that B12 deficiency can present without anaemia or macrocytosis. Neurological and psychiatric symptoms may occur with normal or borderline B12 levels. If clinical suspicion is high, consider therapeutic trial even with normal B12.

B12 Deficiency - Causes and Clinical Presentation

Autoimmune Gastritis (90% of cases)

Intrinsic factor deficiency, anti-parietal cell antibodies positive, lifelong B12 deficiency requiring regular injections.

Malabsorption Syndromes

Crohn's disease, coeliac disease, bacterial overgrowth, post-gastrectomy. May respond to oral high-dose treatment.

Dietary Deficiency

Vegans, strict vegetarians. Usually responds well to oral supplementation if absorption intact.

Drug-Induced

Metformin, PPIs, H2-receptor antagonists, colchicine. May be reversible with cessation or dose reduction.

Step 3: Folate Deficiency - The Forgotten Partner

Always Check B12 Before Treating Folate

Critical safety rule: treating folate deficiency alone when B12 is also deficient can precipitate or worsen neurological complications. Always check both, treat B12 first or simultaneously.

Distinguish Serum vs Red Cell Folate

Serum folate reflects recent intake (days-weeks), red cell folate reflects tissue stores (months). Use red cell folate for definitive diagnosis, especially if dietary intake variable.

Consider Underlying Causes

Folate deficiency is usually dietary, but consider malabsorption, increased demand (pregnancy, haemolysis), drugs (methotrexate, trimethoprim), or alcohol excess.

Monitor Treatment Response

Reticulocyte response occurs within days, Hb rises over weeks. Failure to respond suggests mixed deficiency, ongoing loss, or alternative diagnosis requiring investigation.

Pure Folate Deficiency

Pattern: ‚Üì Folate, Normal B12, Macrocytic anaemia
Causes: Poor diet, malabsorption, increased demand
Treatment: Folic acid 5mg daily for 4 months
Response: Rapid reticulocyte rise, Hb normalizes in 4-6 weeks

Combined B12 and Folate Deficiency

Pattern: ‚Üì Both B12 and Folate, Severe macrocytic anaemia
Risk: Common in malnutrition, alcoholism, malabsorption
Treatment: B12 injections AND folic acid
Caution: Never treat folate alone - can worsen B12 neuropathy

Drug-Induced Folate Deficiency

Common Drugs: Methotrexate, sulfasalazine, trimethoprim
Mechanism: Folate metabolism interference
Prevention: Co-prescribe folic acid with methotrexate
Management: Higher doses may be needed (5-10mg)

Pregnancy-Related Folate Needs

Increased Demand: 4-fold increase in folate requirements
Prevention: 400 ¬µg daily pre-conception and first trimester
High Risk: 5mg daily for previous neural tube defects
Screening: Consider in all pregnant women with anaemia

Clinical Pearl

"The Folate-B12 Safety Dance": Never prescribe folic acid without checking B12 status first. Folate can correct the anaemia of B12 deficiency while allowing neurological damage to progress. This is a potentially irreversible and litigious error - always check both vitamins together.

Folate Deficiency - Special Populations

  • Alcoholics: Poor diet + malabsorption + increased losses - high-dose treatment often needed
  • Pregnancy: Increased demands - all women need 400 ¬µg daily, high-risk need 5mg
  • Haemolysis: Increased red cell turnover increases folate consumption
  • Malabsorption: Coeliac, Crohn's - may need higher doses and longer treatment
  • Elderly: Often combined with B12 deficiency due to poor diet and gastric atrophy

Step 4: Investigation of Underlying Causes

Iron Deficiency - Source Investigation

Men and Post-Menopausal Women:

  • Assume GI blood loss until proven otherwise
  • Upper and lower GI endoscopy if >40 years
  • Urgent 2-week cancer pathway if >60 years
  • Consider coeliac disease, H. pylori

Pre-Menopausal Women:

  • Menstrual history - quantify losses
  • Consider gynaecological causes if heavy periods
  • Still investigate GI if refractory to iron
  • Consider dietary assessment

Special Considerations:

  • NSAIDs, anticoagulants increase bleeding risk
  • Family history of GI cancers
  • Associated symptoms (weight loss, change in bowel habit)

B12 Deficiency - Cause Identification

Autoimmune Gastritis Testing:

  • Anti-intrinsic factor antibodies (specific but only 50% sensitive)
  • Anti-gastric parietal cell antibodies (sensitive but less specific)
  • Consider gastroscopy + biopsy if high suspicion

Malabsorption Assessment:

  • History of GI surgery, especially gastric
  • Symptoms of IBD, coeliac disease
  • Bacterial overgrowth - consider breath test
  • Medication history (PPIs, metformin)

Dietary Assessment:

  • Vegan/vegetarian diet
  • Alcohol intake assessment
  • Socioeconomic factors affecting diet

Clinical Pearl

"Age and Gender Drive Investigation": Iron deficiency in men >50 or post-menopausal women = GI malignancy until proven otherwise. Iron deficiency in pre-menopausal women is usually menstrual, but if it doesn't respond to iron therapy, investigate further. Don't assume all iron deficiency in young women is "normal".

Iron Deficiency Investigation Pathway

Iron deficiency confirmed
Assess risk factors and demographics
Age, gender, menstrual history, symptoms
Men >40 or post-menopausal women: 2-week cancer pathway, upper and lower GI endoscopy
Pre-menopausal women with heavy periods: Treat iron deficiency, gynae assessment if severe
All ages with alarm symptoms: Urgent investigation regardless of demographics

Step 5: Treatment Strategies and Monitoring

Iron Replacement Options and Considerations

Oral Iron Therapy (First-Line)

Options: Ferrous sulfate 200mg BD/TDS, ferrous fumarate 210mg BD
Absorption: Take on empty stomach, avoid with PPIs, tea, coffee
Side Effects: GI upset in 20-30%, constipation common
Duration: Continue 3 months after Hb normalizes to replenish stores

Modified-Release Preparations

Benefits: Reduced GI side effects, better tolerance
Disadvantages: Lower bioavailability, higher cost
Consider for: Patients intolerant of standard preparations
Examples: Ferrograd, Slow-Fe

Intravenous Iron Therapy

Indications: Malabsorption, intolerance, severe deficiency, ongoing losses
Advantages: Rapid replenishment, bypasses absorption issues
Risks: Anaphylaxis (rare), iron overload, cost
Options: Iron carboxymaltose, iron isomaltoside

Treatment Monitoring

Response Check: FBC at 2 weeks (reticulocytes rise first)
Hb Rise: Expect 1 g/dL per week initially
Store Replenishment: Continue 3 months after normalization
No Response: Consider compliance, ongoing loss, wrong diagnosis

B12 and Folate Treatment Protocols

  • B12 with neurological symptoms: 1000 ¬µg IM alternate days until improvement stops, then 2-monthly
  • B12 without neurological symptoms: 1000 ¬µg IM 3x weekly for 2 weeks, then 2-3 monthly
  • Folate deficiency: Folic acid 5mg daily for 4 months (check B12 first!)
  • Prevention in high-risk: Folic acid 400 ¬µg daily (pregnancy), 5mg weekly (methotrexate)
  • Monitoring: Clinical response more important than repeat levels

Clinical Pearl

"The 3-Month Rule": Continue iron therapy for 3 months after Hb normalizes to replenish iron stores. Many patients stop treatment once they feel better, leading to recurrence. Ferritin should rise to >100 ¬µg/L before stopping treatment. This store replenishment phase is crucial for preventing early relapse.

Step 6: Red Flags and Specialist Referral

Immediate Specialist Referral Required

  • Iron deficiency in men >60 or post-menopausal women: 2-week cancer pathway
  • B12 deficiency with neurological symptoms: Urgent haematology/neurology referral
  • Pancytopenia with macrocytosis: Urgent haematology - ?leukaemia/myelodysplasia
  • Iron deficiency not responding to treatment: Consider malabsorption, ongoing loss
  • Very high ferritin (>1000 ¬µg/L) with high TSAT: ?Haemochromatosis/iron overload
  • B12 deficiency in young adults: Investigate underlying cause

Treatment Failure - When to Investigate Further

  • Iron deficiency: No Hb rise after 3 weeks of treatment
  • Possible causes: Poor compliance, malabsorption, ongoing losses, wrong diagnosis
  • B12/Folate: No reticulocyte response after 1 week
  • Consider: Mixed deficiency, myelodysplasia, hypothyroidism
  • Action: Review diagnosis, check compliance, consider IV iron or alternative diagnosis

Clinical Pearl

"When Simple Doesn't Work, Think Complex": Treatment failure in deficiency anaemias usually means: 1) Wrong diagnosis, 2) Mixed deficiencies, 3) Ongoing losses, or 4) Malabsorption. Don't keep increasing doses of the same treatment - step back and reassess the whole clinical picture.

Summary: Mastering Deficiency Anaemia Diagnosis

The Six-Step Deficiency Analysis Method

  1. Pattern Recognition: MCV guides initial thinking - microcytic vs macrocytic
  2. Systematic Testing: Iron studies panel, B12 and folate together
  3. Context Integration: Age, gender, symptoms, comorbidities
  4. Cause Investigation: Why is the patient deficient?
  5. Appropriate Treatment: Right dose, right route, right duration
  6. Response Monitoring: Track response, identify failure early

Master These Patterns

Iron deficiency: Ferritin <15 or TSAT <20%

B12 deficiency: Can present without anaemia

Folate deficiency: Always check B12 first
