Introduction: The Art of Reading Numbers

Every FBC tells a story. The skill lies not in memorising normal ranges, but in recognising patterns, understanding relationships between parameters, and translating numerical data into clinical meaning. This chapter teaches you to read FBC results like a detective examining clues, where each parameter provides evidence about what's happening inside your patient's bone marrow and circulation.

Clinical Pearl

"Never Read in Isolation": A single abnormal value means little - it's the pattern of abnormalities that reveals the diagnosis. Hb 95 g/L with MCV 65 fl tells a completely different story than Hb 95 g/L with MCV 110 fl, even though both represent anaemia.

Step 1: The Systematic Approach to Reading FBC

First Glance: The Overview

Scan quickly - are all three cell lines (red, white, platelets) normal, or are one, two, or all three abnormal? This immediate pattern recognition guides your entire approach.

Red Cell Analysis: Size Matters

If Hb is low, immediately look at MCV. <80 fl = microcytic (think iron deficiency), >100 fl = macrocytic (think B12/folate), 80-100 fl = normocytic (think chronic disease or bleeding).

White Cell Differential: The Detail Story

Don't just look at total WCC - examine the differential. High neutrophils suggest infection, high lymphocytes may indicate viral infection or lymphoproliferative disorder.

Additional Clues: RDW and Blood Film

High RDW indicates variation in cell sizes - valuable in mixed deficiencies. Always request blood film comments when results don't fit the clinical picture.

Clinical Pearl

"The MCV is Your Best Friend": In anaemia, MCV classification is 90% of your diagnosis. Microcytic = iron deficiency until proven otherwise. Macrocytic = B12/folate deficiency until proven otherwise. This simple rule will guide you correctly in the vast majority of cases.

Think: Iron deficiency (95% of cases)

Macrocytic Anaemia (MCV >100 fl)

Classic Pattern: Hb ‚Üì, MCV ‚Üë, often with neutrophil hypersegmentation
Think: B12 or folate deficiency
Next Steps: Check B12 and folate levels
Red Flag: Neurological symptoms suggest B12 deficiency requiring urgent treatment

Normocytic Anaemia (MCV 80-100 fl)

Classic Pattern: Hb ‚Üì, MCV normal, variable RDW
Think: Chronic disease, acute blood loss, or mixed deficiency
Next Steps: Check inflammatory markers, haematinics
Red Flag: Sudden onset suggests acute bleeding

Clinical Pearl

"The RDW Reveals Mixed Stories": Normal MCV with raised RDW (>15%) suggests mixed deficiency - some cells are small (iron deficient) and some large (B12/folate deficient), averaging out to normal. Always check all haematinics when RDW is elevated.

Reading Real Examples

Example 1: Classic Iron Deficiency

Interpretation: Severe microcytic anaemia with high variation - classic iron deficiency pattern

Example 2: B12 Deficiency

Hb 95 g/L, MCV 115 fl, RDW 16%
Interpretation: Macrocytic anaemia - check B12/folate urgently, especially if neurological symptoms

Example 3: Mixed Deficiency

Hb 90 g/L, MCV 88 fl, RDW 20%
Interpretation: Normal MCV but very high RDW suggests both iron and B12/folate deficiency

Step 3: White Cell Interpretation Skills

Reading the White Cell Count

Total WCC alone tells you little - you must examine the differential count to understand what's happening:

  • Neutrophilia (>7.5): Bacterial infection, inflammation, stress response
  • Lymphocytosis (>4.0): Viral infection, or in elderly patients, consider CLL
  • Eosinophilia (>0.4): Allergy, parasites, drug reactions
  • Neutropenia (<2.0): Viral infections, drug-induced, or haematological disorders

Pattern Recognition in WCC

Look for these telling combinations:

  • High WCC + High neutrophils: Bacterial infection
  • High WCC + High lymphocytes: Viral infection or CLL in elderly
  • Low WCC + Low neutrophils: Viral infection or drug effect
  • Very high WCC (>30): Consider leukaemia, request urgent blood film

Clinical Pearl

"Age Changes Everything with Lymphocytosis": Lymphocytosis >5.0 in a patient over 60 is chronic lymphocytic leukaemia until proven otherwise. In younger patients, think viral infection. Same number, completely different meaning based on age.

Step 4: Platelet Count Clinical Significance

Normal Platelets (150-450)

No bleeding risk, normal clotting function. Values at lower end of normal (150-200) still provide adequate haemostasis for most procedures.

Mild Thrombocytopenia (100-150)

Usually no clinical significance. May see prolonged bleeding after trauma or surgery. Most commonly due to viral infections or medications.

Moderate Thrombocytopenia (50-100)

Increased bleeding risk with surgery/procedures. Investigate underlying cause. May need platelet transfusion for major procedures.

Severe Thrombocytopenia (<50)

Spontaneous bleeding risk. Urgent haematology referral required. Patient needs immediate assessment and monitoring.

Clinical Pearl

"Thrombocytosis as a Cancer Clue": Platelets >600 in patients over 60 can be the first sign of underlying malignancy, particularly colorectal or lung cancer. Don't dismiss high platelets as "reactive" - investigate for occult malignancy, especially if persistent.

When Platelet Count Drives Clinical Decisions

  • <50: Hold anticoagulants, urgent haematology referral
  • 50-100: Caution with procedures, investigate cause
  • >600: Consider underlying malignancy in older patients
  • >1000: Paradoxical bleeding risk due to platelet dysfunction

Iron Deficiency Pattern

Hb: Low
MCV: High (>100)
RDW: High
WCC: May be low
Next step: Check B12 and folate

Chronic Disease Anaemia

Hb: Mildly low
MCV: Normal or slightly low
RDW: Normal
Pattern: Everything slightly off

Haematological Malignancy

Pattern: Multiple abnormalities
Hb: Low
WCC: Very high or very low
Platelets: Low
Next step: Urgent blood film and referral

Clinical Pearl

"The Three Cell Line Rule": If all three cell lines (red cells, white cells, platelets) are abnormal, think bone marrow pathology. This is never a coincidence and always requires urgent investigation. Single cell line problems are usually nutritional or reactive.

Step 6: Red Flags That Demand Immediate Action

Results That Require Same-Day Action

  • Hb <70 g/L with symptoms: Consider immediate transfusion needs
  • WCC >50 or <1.0: Phone result to patient immediately, urgent referral
  • Platelets <50: Bleeding precautions, urgent haematology referral
  • Blast cells on film: Same-day haematology discussion
  • Pancytopenia: All three cell lines low - urgent bone marrow investigation needed
  • Any combination of severe abnormalities: Never normal - always investigate urgently

Clinical Pearl

"Trust Your Clinical Instinct": If the FBC result doesn't fit with your clinical assessment of the patient, always request a blood film and consider discussing with haematology. Laboratory errors are rare, but atypical presentations of serious conditions are not.

Summary: Mastering FBC Interpretation

The Five-Step FBC Reading Method

  1. Overview Scan: Normal, one abnormal, or multiple abnormalities?
  2. MCV Classification: If anaemic, let MCV guide your thinking
  3. Pattern Recognition: Look for classic combinations
  4. Clinical Correlation: Does the result fit the patient?
  5. Action Planning: Investigation, treatment, or referral needed?

Master These Patterns

Iron deficiency: Low Hb + Low MCV + High RDW

B12/Folate: Low Hb + High MCV + High RDW

Chronic disease: Mild ‚Üì Hb + Normal MCV

Remember These Rules

MCV is your best diagnostic friend

RDW reveals mixed deficiencies

Multiple abnormalities = bone marrow problem

Never Miss These

Blast cells = urgent haematology

Pancytopenia = bone marrow failure

Extreme values = immediate action

Clinical Pearl

"Practice Makes Pattern Recognition Perfect": The more FBCs you interpret systematically, the faster you'll recognise patterns. Start with MCV in anaemia, examine all three cell lines for abnormalities, and always correlate with clinical picture. These skills will serve you throughout your clinical career.
