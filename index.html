Introduction: Reading the Hormonal Symphony

Imagine each hormone in a sex hormone profile as an instrument in an orchestra. Testosterone might be the bold brass section, estradiol the flowing strings, while LH and FSH conduct the tempo from the pituitary podium. Prolactin occasionally interrupts with an unexpected solo, and SHBG quietly influences the whole performance by controlling how much of each hormone actually reaches its target.

The art lies not in memorising normal ranges, but in listening to the ensemble - recognising when the symphony is harmonious, when instruments are playing out of tune, or when the conductor has lost control entirely. This chapter transforms you from someone who simply reads numbers into a hormonal detective who can hear the story behind the laboratory data.

Clinical Pearl

"Never Trust a Solo Performance": A single hormone level tells you almost nothing - it's the relationships between hormones that reveal the diagnosis. Low testosterone with high LH suggests testicular failure, but low testosterone with low LH points to pituitary problems. Same symptom, opposite locations, completely different treatments.

Red Flag Patterns: When Hormones Scream Emergency

Before we dive into nuanced interpretation, certain hormone patterns demand immediate action. These are not subtle whispers but urgent alarms that should trigger same-day phone calls and rapid referrals.

Prolactin >3000 mIU/L with headaches
Macroadenoma until proven otherwise - urgent MRI and ophthalmology referral for visual field testing
Rapid virilisation in women
New voice changes, male-pattern balding, clitoromegaly - think androgen-secreting tumour, urgent imaging
Testosterone >50 nmol/L in women
Massively elevated androgen suggests tumour rather than PCOS - urgent specialist referral
Young man with very low testosterone + visual symptoms
Pituitary macroadenoma compressing optic chiasm - urgent neurosurgical discussion

Clinical Pearl

"The Hook Effect Trap": When clinical features scream prolactinoma but prolactin is only 'mildly' elevated, suspect the hook effect. Very high prolactin levels can paradoxically give low readings on immunoassays. Ask the lab to dilute and re-test - you might uncover prolactin levels >20,000 mIU/L.

The Detective's Toolkit: What History Reveals

The most sophisticated hormone profile becomes meaningless without the clinical context. Your history-taking needs to be strategic - every question should help you interpret the numbers you're about to receive or have already seen.

Timing Is Everything

  • When did symptoms start? Gradual onset suggests functional causes, rapid changes point to structural problems
  • What time of day was blood taken? Testosterone varies by 40% between morning and evening
  • Which day of cycle? FSH/LH on day 21 are meaningless for ovarian reserve
  • Any recent stress/illness? Can suppress entire HPG axis acutely

Medication Archaeology

  • Contraceptive pills/patches: Raise SHBG, lower free testosterone
  • Antipsychotics/antiemetics: Block dopamine, raise prolactin
  • Opioids (including codeine): Suppress LH/FSH centrally
  • Glucocorticoids: Shut down hypothalamic drive

Step 1: Set the Scene

Age, sex, reproductive goals, current medications. Are we dealing with primary amenorrhoea, infertility, erectile dysfunction, or something else entirely?

Step 2: Timeline the Trouble

When did symptoms start? Has libido gradually declined over years (think age-related) or crashed in months (think pituitary adenoma)?

Step 3: Map the Medications

Current and recent drugs that affect the HPG axis. Include herbal supplements, anabolic steroids, and recreational drugs.

Step 4: Hunt for Clues

Galactorrhoea, visual field defects, hot flushes, loss of morning erections, changes in body hair - each symptom points to specific hormone disruptions.

Clinical Pearl

"The Stress Test Question": Always ask 'How's your sleep, stress, and exercise routine?' Over-training, chronic stress, and sleep deprivation can mimic hypogonadism perfectly. Fix the lifestyle before you prescribe the hormones.

The LH/FSH Detective Story: Primary vs Secondary

LH and FSH are your diagnostic compass - they always point toward the problem. Think of them as messengers from the pituitary: if they're shouting loudly (high levels) but getting no response from the gonads, you have primary gonadal failure. If they're whispering quietly (low levels) despite obvious gonadal problems, the fault lies centrally.

High LH/FSH Pattern

Typical Values: LH >15, FSH >15
Meaning: Pituitary shouting at unresponsive gonads
Causes: Testicular failure, ovarian failure, menopause
Action: Focus on gonadal causes, consider HRT/TRT

Low/Normal LH/FSH Pattern

Typical Values: LH <2, FSH <3 with low sex steroids
Meaning: Central suppression or dysfunction
Causes: Pituitary adenoma, medications, chronic illness
Action: MRI pituitary, review medications, check prolactin

High LH/FSH Ratio (>2.5)

Typical Pattern: LH 15, FSH 6 in women
Meaning: PCOS phenotype likely
Context: Irregular cycles, hirsutism, obesity
Action: Metabolic assessment, androgen levels, pelvic ultrasound

Very High FSH (>30)

Typical Pattern: FSH 45, LH 25 in young woman
Meaning: Primary ovarian insufficiency
Urgency: High - fertility and bone health implications
Action: Urgent gynae referral, genetic counselling, bone protection

Clinical Pearl

"The PCOS Ratio Trap": Not all women with PCOS have a raised LH/FSH ratio - about 40% have normal ratios. But if you see LH/FSH >2.5 with irregular periods, PCOS is highly likely. Use the ratio as a clue, not a rule-out test.

Prolactin: The Great Disruptor

Prolactin is like that colleague who's normally quiet but occasionally dominates the entire meeting. When prolactin is elevated, it suppresses GnRH pulsatility, effectively silencing LH and FSH. This is why hyperprolactinaemia presents with hypogonadal symptoms despite normal-looking gonads.

Normal Prolactin (<600 mIU/L)

Allows normal pulsatile GnRH release. LH and FSH can respond appropriately to feedback. Normal reproductive function expected.

Mildly Elevated (600-1000)

Often medication-induced (antipsychotics, SSRIs). May cause subtle menstrual irregularities. Review medications before investigating further.

Moderately Elevated (1000-3000)

Significant GnRH suppression likely. Check for pregnancy, hypothyroidism. Consider macroprolactin. May need MRI if persistent.

Markedly Elevated (>3000)

Prolactinoma likely. Complete suppression of reproductive axis. Urgent MRI and ophthalmology referral. Rule out hook effect with dilutional assay.

Clinical Pearl

"The Macroprolactin Red Herring": About 15% of elevated prolactin is actually macroprolactin - a biologically inactive complex that cross-reacts in the assay. Always check if your lab routinely screens for this, as it prevents unnecessary imaging and anxiety.

Common Causes of Hyperprolactinaemia by Level

  • 600-1000 mIU/L: Medications, stress, nipple stimulation, hypothyroidism
  • 1000-3000 mIU/L: Microprolactinoma, stalk compression, kidney disease
  • >3000 mIU/L: Prolactinoma (micro or macro), pregnancy, drugs (rare to cause this level)
  • >10,000 mIU/L: Almost always prolactinoma - but watch for hook effect

SHBG: The Invisible Modifier

SHBG is like a sponge that soaks up hormones, determining how much actually reaches target tissues. High SHBG makes total testosterone levels look better than they functionally are, while low SHBG makes them look worse. It's the lens through which you must view all sex steroid measurements.

"SHBG is Your Reality Check": Never interpret testosterone without knowing SHBG. A man with total testosterone of 10 nmol/L might be hypogonadal if SHBG is high (not enough free hormone) or perfectly normal if SHBG is low (plenty of free hormone available).

Pattern Recognition: Common Clinical Scenarios

The Tired Middle-Aged Man

Pattern: Total T 8-12 nmol/L, normal/low LH/FSH, normal prolactin
Often: High BMI, sleep apnea, diabetes
Think: Functional hypogonadism vs age-related decline
Action: Lifestyle first, repeat hormones, consider CPAP

Young Woman with Irregular Periods

Pattern: LH/FSH >2.5, low SHBG, elevated androgens
Often: Weight gain, hirsutism, acanthosis nigricans
Think: PCOS with metabolic features
Action: Metabolic assessment, lifestyle, metformin consideration

Woman with Hot Flushes at 42

Pattern: High FSH (>30), low estradiol, irregular periods
Often: Family history, autoimmune conditions
Think: Primary ovarian insufficiency
Action: Urgent referral, genetic counselling, bone protection, HRT discussion

Bodybuilder with Mood Changes

Pattern: Low/undetectable LH/FSH, variable testosterone
Often: Recent cessation of anabolic steroids
Think: Suppressed HPG axis recovery
Action: Time, supportive care, avoid immediate TRT

Clinical Pearl

"Context Trumps Numbers": The same hormone profile can mean completely different things in different people. Low testosterone with suppressed LH/FSH in a 25-year-old bodybuilder needs time to recover, but the same pattern in a 45-year-old with headaches needs urgent pituitary imaging.

Treatment Monitoring: Following the Thread

Once treatment starts, the hormone profile transforms from diagnostic tool to monitoring device. Each therapy creates its own signature pattern, and understanding these helps you optimise treatment and spot problems early.

Testosterone Replacement in Men

Expect suppressed LH/FSH (negative feedback working). Monitor trough testosterone levels. If persistently low despite good adherence, check SHBG and consider absorption issues.

Dopamine Agonists for Prolactinomas

Prolactin should fall dramatically within weeks. LH/FSH recovery may take months. If prolactin normalises but symptoms persist, look for other causes of hypogonadism.

Clomiphene for Hypogonadism

Should raise LH/FSH and consequently testosterone. If LH/FSH rise but testosterone doesn't, suggests primary testicular problem. If neither rise, suggests central resistance.

Metformin in PCOS

May lower LH/FSH ratio over months. SHBG may rise (good sign). Androgen levels should improve. Weight loss amplifies hormonal improvements.

Red Flags During Treatment

  • Rising prolactin on dopamine agonist: Non-compliance, resistance, or wrong diagnosis
  • Persistently suppressed LH on clomiphene: Inadequate dose or central resistance
  • Hematocrit >54% on TRT: Increased stroke risk, reduce dose or stop
  • Worsening virilisation on testosterone therapy in women: Overdosing or poor compliance with application instructions

Summary: The Five-Step Hormone Interpretation Method

Your Systematic Approach to Any Sex Hormone Profile

  1. Context First: Age, symptoms, medications, timing of sample - set the clinical scene before looking at numbers
  2. Follow the Feedback Loop: Pair each sex hormone with its pituitary driver - discordance localises the problem
  3. Adjust for SHBG: Calculate free hormone indices when total levels don't match clinical picture
  4. Check the Disruptors: Elevated prolactin explains many mysterious patterns of hypogonadism
  5. Integrate and Act: Combine hormone patterns with clinical features to guide investigation and treatment
