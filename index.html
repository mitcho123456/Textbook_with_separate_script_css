Introduction: The Hormones That Shape Lives

Every sex hormone profile you order tells a deeply personal story. Behind those numbers are patients struggling with fertility, teenagers questioning their development, middle-aged people facing unexpected changes, and older adults navigating life transitions. The skill isn't just knowing what the numbers mean‚Äîit's understanding which story those numbers are telling and what that means for the human sitting in front of you.

Sex hormones don't work in isolation. They're part of an intricate conversation between the brain, ovaries or testes, and every tissue in the body. When you learn to read these profiles properly, you're not just interpreting lab values‚Äîyou're becoming fluent in the language the body uses to communicate about reproduction, development, and aging.

Clinical Pearl

"Context Is King": A testosterone level of 10 nmol/L means completely different things in a 16-year-old boy (concerning), a 30-year-old woman with hirsutism (diagnostic), and a 70-year-old man (potentially normal for age). Never interpret hormone levels without considering age, sex, timing, and clinical context.

Step 1: Before You Order - The Art of Targeted Testing

Define Your Question

Don't order a "hormone panel" without a clear clinical question. Are you investigating fertility, checking for PCOS, evaluating delayed puberty, or exploring menopausal symptoms? Your question determines which tests to order and when.

Timing Matters Hugely

Testosterone: early morning (8-10am) when levels peak. For women: consider menstrual cycle phase. FSH/LH: day 2-5 of cycle for baseline. Oestradiol: depends on your question‚Äîfollicular phase for baseline, mid-cycle for ovulation assessment.

Consider Current Medications

Oral contraceptives dramatically alter hormone levels‚Äîstop 3 months before testing if possible. HRT, steroids, anticonvulsants, and many psychiatric medications affect results. Don't waste money testing while on hormonal contraception.

Clinical Context First

What does the patient look like? A hirsute woman with acne and irregular periods has PCOS until proven otherwise, regardless of what the lab says. Clinical features often trump individual hormone levels.

Clinical Pearl

"The Morning Rule": Testosterone levels can drop by 30-40% from morning to afternoon due to natural circadian rhythm. A "low" afternoon testosterone might be completely normal if taken in the morning. Always order testosterone before 10am, especially in men under 50.

Testing on the Pill
Hormone levels on combined oral contraceptives are meaningless‚Äîyou're measuring the pill's effects, not the patient's natural production
Random Cycle Testing
Ordering hormones at random cycle points in reproductive-age women often creates more confusion than clarity
Afternoon Testosterone in Men
Late-day testosterone in younger men is notoriously unreliable‚Äîstick to morning samples or risk misdiagnosis
Single FSH for Menopause
FSH fluctuates wildly in perimenopause‚Äîone normal result doesn't exclude menopausal transition

Step 2: Decoding Male Hormone Patterns

Normal Male Pattern

Testosterone: 12-28 nmol/L (morning)
LH: 2-8 U/L
FSH: 1-8 U/L
Interpretation: Healthy hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular axis. Everything working as it should.

Primary Hypogonadism

Testosterone: Low (<8 nmol/L)
LH/FSH: High (>12 U/L)
Think: Testicular failure
Causes: Klinefelter's, mumps orchitis, chemotherapy, trauma

Secondary Hypogonadism

Testosterone: Low (<8 nmol/L)
LH/FSH: Low/normal (<5 U/L)
Think: Brain problem (pituitary/hypothalamic)
Causes: Prolactinoma, obesity, sleep apnea, stress

Age-Related Decline

Testosterone: Borderline (8-12 nmol/L)
LH/FSH: Normal to slightly high
Think: Natural aging process
Action: Symptoms matter more than numbers in older men

Clinical Pearl

"The LH Tells the Story": In men with low testosterone, LH reveals the cause. High LH means the brain is screaming at failed testicles (primary hypogonadism). Low/normal LH means the brain itself isn't working (secondary hypogonadism). Same low testosterone, completely different causes and treatments.

Step 3: Decoding Female Hormone Patterns

Reproductive Years: It's All About Timing

Female hormone interpretation is like reading a constantly changing script. The same woman can have completely different hormone levels depending on where she is in her cycle:

  • Follicular phase (days 1-7): Low oestradiol, rising FSH
  • Mid-cycle (days 12-16): Oestradiol surge, LH peak
  • Luteal phase (days 15-28): High progesterone, falling oestradiol

This is why random hormone testing in women often creates more confusion than clarity. Know when you're sampling, or the results are meaningless.

The PCOS Pattern: Learning to Spot It

PCOS is the most common hormone disorder you'll see in women of reproductive age, affecting 10-13% of women. The pattern is distinctive:

  • Testosterone: Elevated (>2.5 nmol/L)
  • Free androgen index: >5
  • LH:FSH ratio: Often >2:1 (but not diagnostic)
  • SHBG: Often low due to insulin resistance

Remember: clinical signs (hirsutism, acne, irregular periods) plus elevated androgens equals PCOS in most cases.

Clinical Pearl

"The Free Androgen Index Trick": Can't get free testosterone measured? Calculate free androgen index: (total testosterone √ó 100) √∑ SHBG. Values >5 suggest PCOS. It's not perfect, but it's widely available and clinically useful for detecting hyperandrogenism.

Step 4: The Menopause Maze - Navigating Transition

NICE 2024 Guidelines: Menopause Diagnosis Made Simple

Over 45 with typical symptoms: Don't test‚Äîdiagnose clinically based on symptoms and menstrual changes. Save your lab budget for cases that matter.

Ages 40-45: Consider FSH if diagnosis unclear, but remember it fluctuates wildly in perimenopause.

Under 40: Always test if menopause suspected‚Äîpremature ovarian insufficiency has serious health implications.

Understanding Menopausal Hormone Patterns

Premenopause

FSH: <10 U/L

Oestradiol: Cyclic variation

Periods: Regular

Perimenopause

FSH: 15-40 U/L (variable)

Oestradiol: Erratic levels

Periods: Irregular, changing

Postmenopause

FSH: >30 U/L consistently

"The Perimenopause Paradox": A woman can have a normal FSH on Monday and menopausal levels on Friday. During perimenopause, hormones fluctuate wildly, making single hormone tests unreliable. Trust the clinical picture‚Äîirregular periods and vasomotor symptoms trump lab values.

Step 5: Putting It All Together - Pattern Recognition

Start with the Clinical Picture

What does the patient look like? What are their symptoms? A 25-year-old woman with hirsutism and acne probably has PCOS regardless of the exact hormone numbers. Clinical features guide interpretation, not the other way around.

Look for the Primary Pattern

High hormones with low LH/FSH = tumor or external source. Low hormones with high LH/FSH = gland failure. Low hormones with low LH/FSH = brain problem. This fundamental pattern tells you where the problem is.

Consider the Context

Age, medications, timing, and clinical presentation all matter enormously. The same hormone levels can be normal, concerning, or diagnostic depending on context. Never interpret numbers in isolation.

Know When to Refer

Complex patterns, tumor-level hormones, discordant clinical and biochemical findings, or when you're unsure‚Äîthese all warrant specialist input. Don't struggle alone with confusing results.

Rapid virilization

Discordant findings

Don't Test If:

Clear clinical diagnosis
Wouldn't change management
Menopause >45 with symptoms

Remember to Consider:

Sample timing
Patient age

Clinical context

Clinical Pearl

"The Most Important Test": Your clinical assessment matters more than any single hormone level. If the biochemistry doesn't fit the clinical picture, trust your clinical judgment and either retest or refer. Laboratories make mistakes, assays have limitations, and patients are complex.

Step 6: Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

The Afternoon Testosterone Trap

Mistake: Ordering testosterone any time of day
Reality: Levels drop 30-40% by afternoon
Solution: Always before 10am, especially in younger men
Exception: Less critical in older men with lower amplitude variation

The Contraceptive Confusion

Mistake: Testing hormones on the pill
Reality: You're measuring pill effects, not natural production
Solution: Stop hormones 3 months before testing
Exception: Not always practical‚Äîdiscuss with patient first

The Single FSH Fallacy

Mistake: One normal FSH rules out menopause
Reality: FSH fluctuates wildly in perimenopause
Solution: Diagnose clinically over 45, repeat if under 45
Remember: Symptoms matter more than single values

The Reference Range Trap

Mistake: Normal range = normal for this patient
Reality: Ranges are population-based, not individualized
Solution: Consider age, sex, and clinical context always
Example: FSH 15 U/L is normal at 50, concerning at 25

The "Always Remember" List for Hormone Testing

  • Timing is everything: Wrong timing can make normal look abnormal and vice versa
  • One abnormal result isn't a diagnosis: Confirm unexpected findings with repeat testing
  • Medications matter: Many drugs affect hormone levels‚Äîcheck current medications
  • Age changes everything: The same level can be normal at one age, abnormal at another
  • Clinical correlation is key: If it doesn't fit, don't force it‚Äîrecheck or refer
  • Stress affects results: Acute illness, psychological stress, and extreme exercise alter levels

Summary: Mastering Hormone Interpretation

The Five-Step Hormone Reading Method

1. Clinical Context

Age, symptoms, timing, medications - these shape interpretation more than numbers

2. Pattern Recognition

High/low hormones with high/low LH/FSH reveals the site of pathology

3. Physiological Sense

Does this result make biological sense given the patient's presentation?

4. Confirmatory Testing

Unexpected results need confirmation - don't change management on single tests

5. Clinical Decision

What does this mean for the patient? How does it change management?

What to Remember About Each Hormone

Testosterone: Morning samples essential in men, context crucial in women. Free testosterone more accurate than total.

LH/FSH: The brain's messengers - high levels mean the brain is trying to stimulate failed gonads, low levels suggest brain problems.

Oestradiol: Fluctuates dramatically with menstrual cycle. Timing is everything for interpretation.

SHBG: The carrier protein that affects free hormone levels. High SHBG lowers free hormones, low SHBG raises them.

The Most Common Patterns You'll See

PCOS: High testosterone + clinical signs + irregular periods

Male hypogonadism: Low testosterone - check LH to find the cause

Menopause: Clinical diagnosis over 45, FSH confirmation under 40

Premature ovarian insufficiency: High FSH + low oestradiol under age 40

Clinical Pearl

"The Human Touch in Hormone Medicine": Behind every hormone profile is a person with hopes, fears, and concerns about their body and future. Whether they're struggling with fertility, worried about their development, or navigating life changes, remember that these numbers represent deeply personal experiences. Your skill in interpreting these results can provide answers, reassurance, and direction at crucial moments in their lives.

Take Home Messages for Hormone Interpretation

  • Context trumps numbers: Clinical picture matters more than isolated lab values
  • Timing is critical: Wrong timing can completely mislead interpretation
  • Patterns tell stories: Look for the overall pattern, not individual values
  • Confirm unexpected findings: One abnormal result doesn't make a diagnosis
  • Know when to refer: Complex cases need specialist input
  • Remember the human: These are deeply personal results affecting real lives

Introduction: Reading the Hormonal Symphony

Imagine each hormone in a sex hormone profile as an instrument in an orchestra. Testosterone might be the bold brass section, estradiol the flowing strings, while LH and FSH conduct the tempo from the pituitary podium. Prolactin occasionally interrupts with an unexpected solo, and SHBG quietly influences the whole performance by controlling how much of each hormone actually reaches its target.

The art lies not in memorising normal ranges, but in listening to the ensemble - recognising when the symphony is harmonious, when instruments are playing out of tune, or when the conductor has lost control entirely. This chapter transforms you from someone who simply reads numbers into a hormonal detective who can hear the story behind the laboratory data.

Clinical Pearl

"Never Trust a Solo Performance": A single hormone level tells you almost nothing - it's the relationships between hormones that reveal the diagnosis. Low testosterone with high LH suggests testicular failure, but low testosterone with low LH points to pituitary problems. Same symptom, opposite locations, completely different treatments.

Red Flag Patterns: When Hormones Scream Emergency

Before we dive into nuanced interpretation, certain hormone patterns demand immediate action. These are not subtle whispers but urgent alarms that should trigger same-day phone calls and rapid referrals.

Prolactin >3000 mIU/L with headaches
Macroadenoma until proven otherwise - urgent MRI and ophthalmology referral for visual field testing
Rapid virilisation in women
New voice changes, male-pattern balding, clitoromegaly - think androgen-secreting tumour, urgent imaging
Testosterone >50 nmol/L in women
Massively elevated androgen suggests tumour rather than PCOS - urgent specialist referral
Young man with very low testosterone + visual symptoms

Clinical Pearl

"The Hook Effect Trap": When clinical features scream prolactinoma but prolactin is only 'mildly' elevated, suspect the hook effect. Very high prolactin levels can paradoxically give low readings on immunoassays. Ask the lab to dilute and re-test - you might uncover prolactin levels >20,000 mIU/L.

The Detective's Toolkit: What History Reveals

The most sophisticated hormone profile becomes meaningless without the clinical context. Your history-taking needs to be strategic - every question should help you interpret the numbers you're about to receive or have already seen.

Timing Is Everything

  • When did symptoms start? Gradual onset suggests functional causes, rapid changes point to structural problems
  • What time of day was blood taken? Testosterone varies by 40% between morning and evening
  • Which day of cycle? FSH/LH on day 21 are meaningless for ovarian reserve
  • Any recent stress/illness? Can suppress entire HPG axis acutely

Medication Archaeology

  • Contraceptive pills/patches: Raise SHBG, lower free testosterone
  • Antipsychotics/antiemetics: Block dopamine, raise prolactin
  • Opioids (including codeine): Suppress LH/FSH centrally
  • Glucocorticoids: Shut down hypothalamic drive

Step 1: Set the Scene

Age, sex, reproductive goals, current medications. Are we dealing with primary amenorrhoea, infertility, erectile dysfunction, or something else entirely?

Step 2: Timeline the Trouble

When did symptoms start? Has libido gradually declined over years (think age-related) or crashed in months (think pituitary adenoma)?

Step 3: Map the Medications

Current and recent drugs that affect the HPG axis. Include herbal supplements, anabolic steroids, and recreational drugs.

Step 4: Hunt for Clues

Galactorrhoea, visual field defects, hot flushes, loss of morning erections, changes in body hair - each symptom points to specific hormone disruptions.

Clinical Pearl

"The Stress Test Question": Always ask 'How's your sleep, stress, and exercise routine?' Over-training, chronic stress, and sleep deprivation can mimic hypogonadism perfectly. Fix the lifestyle before you prescribe the hormones.

The LH/FSH Detective Story: Primary vs Secondary

LH and FSH are your diagnostic compass - they always point toward the problem. Think of them as messengers from the pituitary: if they're shouting loudly (high levels) but getting no response from the gonads, you have primary gonadal failure. If they're whispering quietly (low levels) despite obvious gonadal problems, the fault lies centrally.

High LH/FSH Pattern

Typical Values: LH >15, FSH >15
Meaning: Pituitary shouting at unresponsive gonads
Causes: Testicular failure, ovarian failure, menopause
Action: Focus on gonadal causes, consider HRT/TRT

Low/Normal LH/FSH Pattern

Typical Values: LH <2, FSH <3 with low sex steroids
Meaning: Central suppression or dysfunction
Causes: Pituitary adenoma, medications, chronic illness
Action: MRI pituitary, review medications, check prolactin

High LH/FSH Ratio (>2.5)

Typical Pattern: LH 15, FSH 6 in women
Meaning: PCOS phenotype likely
Context: Irregular cycles, hirsutism, obesity
Action: Metabolic assessment, androgen levels, pelvic ultrasound

Very High FSH (>30)

Typical Pattern: FSH 45, LH 25 in young woman
Meaning: Primary ovarian insufficiency
Urgency: High - fertility and bone health implications
Action: Urgent gynae referral, genetic counselling, bone protection

Clinical Pearl

"The PCOS Ratio Trap": Not all women with PCOS have a raised LH/FSH ratio - about 40% have normal ratios. But if you see LH/FSH >2.5 with irregular periods, PCOS is highly likely. Use the ratio as a clue, not a rule-out test.

Prolactin: The Great Disruptor

Prolactin is like that colleague who's normally quiet but occasionally dominates the entire meeting. When prolactin is elevated, it suppresses GnRH pulsatility, effectively silencing LH and FSH. This is why hyperprolactinaemia presents with hypogonadal symptoms despite normal-looking gonads.

Normal Prolactin (<600 mIU/L)

Allows normal pulsatile GnRH release. LH and FSH can respond appropriately to feedback. Normal reproductive function expected.

Mildly Elevated (600-1000)

Often medication-induced (antipsychotics, SSRIs). May cause subtle menstrual irregularities. Review medications before investigating further.

Moderately Elevated (1000-3000)

Significant GnRH suppression likely. Check for pregnancy, hypothyroidism. Consider macroprolactin. May need MRI if persistent.

Markedly Elevated (>3000)

Prolactinoma likely. Complete suppression of reproductive axis. Urgent MRI and ophthalmology referral. Rule out hook effect with dilutional assay.

Clinical Pearl

"The Macroprolactin Red Herring": About 15% of elevated prolactin is actually macroprolactin - a biologically inactive complex that cross-reacts in the assay. Always check if your lab routinely screens for this, as it prevents unnecessary imaging and anxiety.

Common Causes of Hyperprolactinaemia by Level

  • 600-1000 mIU/L: Medications, stress, nipple stimulation, hypothyroidism
  • 1000-3000 mIU/L: Microprolactinoma, stalk compression, kidney disease
  • >3000 mIU/L: Prolactinoma (micro or macro), pregnancy, drugs (rare to cause this level)
  • >10,000 mIU/L: Almost always prolactinoma - but watch for hook effect

Treatment Monitoring: Following the Thread

Once treatment starts, the hormone profile transforms from diagnostic tool to monitoring device. Each therapy creates its own signature pattern, and understanding these helps you optimise treatment and spot problems early.

Testosterone Replacement in Men

Expect suppressed LH/FSH (negative feedback working). Monitor trough testosterone levels. If persistently low despite good adherence, check SHBG and consider absorption issues.

Dopamine Agonists for Prolactinomas

Prolactin should fall dramatically within weeks. LH/FSH recovery may take months. If prolactin normalises but symptoms persist, look for other causes of hypogonadism.

Clomiphene for Hypogonadism

Should raise LH/FSH and consequently testosterone. If LH/FSH rise but testosterone doesn't, suggests primary testicular problem. If neither rise, suggests central resistance.

Metformin in PCOS

May lower LH/FSH ratio over months. SHBG may rise (good sign). Androgen levels should improve. Weight loss amplifies hormonal improvements.

Red Flags During Treatment

  • Rising prolactin on dopamine agonist: Non-compliance, resistance, or wrong diagnosis
  • Persistently suppressed LH on clomiphene: Inadequate dose or central resistance
  • Hematocrit >54% on TRT: Increased stroke risk, reduce dose or stop
  • Worsening virilisation on testosterone therapy in women: Overdosing or poor compliance with application instructions

Your Systematic Approach to Any Sex Hormone Profile

  2. Follow the Feedback Loop: Pair each sex hormone with its pituitary driver - discordance localises the problem
  3. Adjust for SHBG: Calculate free hormone indices when total levels don't match clinical picture
  4. Check the Disruptors: Elevated prolactin explains many mysterious patterns of hypogonadism
  5. Integrate and Act: Combine hormone patterns with clinical features to guide investigation and treatment
