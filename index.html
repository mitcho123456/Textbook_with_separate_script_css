Introduction: Turning Numbers into Clinical Stories

The Full Blood Count (FBC) is a high‚Äëyield test that reflects marrow function and peripheral events across three cell lines‚Äîred cells, white cells, and platelets. Interpretation is strongest when numerical data, blood film morphology, and clinical context are integrated. Read for patterns, not isolated values.

Clinical Pearl

"Triple Assessment": Always assess red cells (anaemia), white cells (infection/inflammation), and platelets (bleeding/clotting risk) in sequence. Patterns across lines often reveal marrow pathology.

First Glance & Red Flags

Overview Scan

Are one, two, or all three cell lines abnormal? Multi‚Äëlineage changes ‚Üí think bone marrow pathology; isolated changes ‚Üí often reactive or nutritional.

Systematic Method: Five Steps

1) Context

Age, sex, pregnancy, comorbidities, meds. Adjust expectations (e.g., children, pregnant patients) and correlate with presentation.

2) Red Cells

If anaemic, let MCV drive the differential: microcytic (<80 fL), normocytic (80‚Äì100 fL), macrocytic (>100 fL). Add reticulocytes early.

3) White Cells

Total WCC + differential (neutrophils, lymphocytes, eosinophils, monocytes). Look for left shift/toxic granulation.

4) Platelets

Count + MPV; beware pseudothrombocytopenia (EDTA clumping). Consider reactive vs clonal causes.

5) Pattern Recognition

Cross‚Äëline patterns (e.g., anaemia + thrombocytosis in chronic disease; pancytopenia in marrow failure).

Clinical Pearl

MCV & RDW are high‚Äëyield: MCV classifies; RDW (>15%) suggests mixed populations or evolving change.

Anaemia by MCV: Diagnose with Purpose

Microcytic (<80 fL)

TAILS: Thalassaemia/haemoglobinopathies, Anaemia of chronic disease, Iron deficiency (most common), Lead (rare), Sideroblastic.

  • Pattern: Hb ‚Üì, MCV ‚Üì, RDW ‚Üë, platelets often ‚Üë (reactive).
  • Next: Ferritin, transferrin saturation; consider GI loss (men >50, post‚Äëmenopausal women).

Normocytic (80‚Äì100 fL)

  • High reticulocytes ‚Üí haemolysis or acute blood loss.
  • Low/normal reticulocytes ‚Üí chronic disease, renal disease, marrow failure.

Macrocytic (>100 fL)

  • Megaloblastic: B12/folate deficiency (¬± hypersegmented neutrophils).
  • Non‚Äëmegaloblastic: Alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, reticulocytosis.

B12 deficiency may present with neurological features (check methylmalonic acid if unclear).

Platelets: Bleeding & Clotting Risk

Thrombocytopenia

Bleeding Risk by Count

  • >50 √ó10‚Åπ/L: usually asymptomatic
  • 30‚Äì50 √ó10‚Åπ/L: bleeding with trauma
  • 10‚Äì30 √ó10‚Åπ/L: spontaneous bruising/bleeding
  • <10 √ó10‚Åπ/L: serious bleeding risk (emergency)

Mechanisms

  • ‚Üì Production: B12/folate deficiency, chemo, marrow infiltration
  • ‚Üë Destruction: ITP, TTP, HIT, hypersplenism
  • Sequestration: Splenomegaly
  • Dilution: Massive transfusion

Summary & Checklist

Essential Steps

  • Screen for red flags & critical values.
  • Classify anaemia by MCV; use RDW & retics.
  • Interrogate differential WCC.
  • Assess platelets & MPV; exclude clumping artefact.
  • Recognise multi‚Äëlineage patterns.
  • Trend over time; correlate clinically.

Never Miss

  • Blasts, leucoerythroblastic films.
  • Pancytopenia or extreme counts.
  • Persistent thrombocytosis in older adults.
  • Mixed deficiency signalled by high RDW.

Bottom Line

1. Be systematic. Read all three lines every time.

2. Context rules. Numbers + patient story + film.

3. Trend and act. Repeat if uncertain; refer early for multi‚Äëlineage or rapidly progressive abnormalities.
