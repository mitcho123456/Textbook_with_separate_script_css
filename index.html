Structured History Framework

The history supplies the physiologic context that numbers cannot. Use the prompts below; each question is anchored to a mechanism so you and your patient can co‚Äëreason through the findings.

Symptoms & Timelines

  • Libido, erectile function, menstrual regularity: track hypothalamic‚Äìpituitary‚Äìgonadal (HPG) output over time; chronicity suggests long‚Äëstanding axis change.
  • Hot flushes, night sweats, sleep disruption: vasomotor instability from low estradiol/testosterone; also consider thyroid and medication effects.
  • Rapid change vs gradual drift: tumours and iatrogenic causes change quickly; metabolic or functional causes drift.
  • Fertility goals now/later: alters thresholds for investigation and urgency.

Exposures & Comorbidities

  • Medications: antipsychotics/antiemetics (‚Üëprolactin), opioids (‚ÜìGnRH), glucocorticoids (central suppression), oral estrogens (‚ÜëSHBG).
  • Metabolic state: obesity/insulin resistance (‚ÜìSHBG, ‚Üëfree androgens), eating disorders/over‚Äëexercise (hypothalamic amenorrhoea).
  • Sleep apnoea, alcohol, thyroid, liver/renal disease: common modulators of the axis and of SHBG synthesis.
  • Androgen or estrogen therapy, fertility treatment, anabolic steroids: interpret all results through the lens of exogenous hormones.

Why We Ask

Each question links to a feedback step: hypothalamus (GnRH pulsatility), pituitary (LH/FSH), gonads (sex steroids), and binding (SHBG). When symptoms, exposure, and numbers line up, your probability of the correct diagnosis rises sharply.

Clinical Decision Points

The following flow models translate patterns into actions. Follow the arrows, but let the history steer the speed.

1) Is the sex steroid low (for age/sex) on a correctly timed sample?

If borderline, repeat with proper timing and include SHBG to allow free/bioavailable calculation.

2) What do LH/FSH say?

High LH/FSH: primary gonadal failure (testes/ovaries not responding).
Low or inappropriately normal LH/FSH: central (pituitary/hypothalamic) cause or functional suppression.

3) Where does SHBG point?

High SHBG (oral estrogens, hyperthyroidism, liver disease) can mask low free T; low SHBG (obesity/insulin resistance) inflates free T‚Äîrecalculate.

4) Is prolactin interfering?

Raised prolactin suppresses GnRH ‚Üí low LH/FSH; consider medication causes first, then pituitary imaging if marked/persistent.

5) Align with the story

Do the numbers fit the person? If not, expand the frame (thyroid, cortisol, iron studies, coeliac screen, sleep apnoea, metabolic risk).

Low Testosterone + ‚ÜëLH/FSH

Primary testicular failure (e.g., Klinefelter, post‚Äëorchitis/chemo/radiation). Assess fertility, bone, and cardiometabolic risks; refer as appropriate.

Low Testosterone + ‚ÜòLH/FSH

Secondary hypogonadism (pituitary/hypothalamic, functional suppression from illness/opioids/obesity). Screen meds, prolactin, pituitary symptoms.

Elevated Prolactin + Low/Normal LH/FSH

Hyperprolactinaemia with gonadotrophin suppression. Check drugs, pregnancy, hypothyroidism; if marked/persistent, discuss imaging and endocrine referral.

Women: High Androgens + Low SHBG

Insulin resistance and PCOS phenotype likely; address metabolic drivers and cycle control; exclude late‚Äëonset CAH if features suggest.

Women: Low Estradiol + High FSH

Primary ovarian insufficiency‚Äîdiscuss fertility impact, bone protection, and indications for HRT.

Women: Oligo‚ÄëAmenorrhoea + Normal Androgens

Consider functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea (energy deficit, stress); counsel on nutrition, training load, and bone health.

Treatment History Insights

Therapy reshapes physiology. Document what (agent and route), when (timing to blood draw), and why (indication and goals), then interpret through that prism.

Exogenous Testosterone (men)

Expect suppressed LH/FSH. Judge adequacy using symptoms + trough levels (timed to formulation). Persisting low values ‚Üí adherence, absorption, or SHBG issues.

Combined Oral Contraceptive / Oral Estrogen

Raises SHBG ‚Üí lowers free T despite normal total; androgen measures off therapy are more informative when assessing hyperandrogenism.

Dopamine Agonists (hyperprolactinaemia)

Prolactin should fall; restoration of gonadal function is the clinical target. If prolactin normalises but symptoms persist, re‚Äëexamine other axes.

Antipsychotics / Metoclopramide

Can elevate prolactin substantially; collaborate with prescriber on risk‚Äìbenefit and alternatives if hypogonadal symptoms are significant.

Clinical Pearl

Summary: The Thinking Model on One Page

Five Moves to Master

  1. Time it right: record time, fasting, and (if relevant) cycle day; repeat borderline values.
  2. Read the duet: interpret sex steroid alongside LH/FSH to localise primary vs central.
  3. Adjust the lens: use SHBG to judge free/bioavailable hormone before labelling deficiency.
  4. Unmask prolactin: check drugs, pregnancy, thyroid; pursue imaging if marked/persistent.
  5. Match the story: align with symptoms, meds, and goals; manage risks (bone, cardiometabolic, fertility).

  • Female hirsutism + irregular cycles + low SHBG ‚Üí insulin resistance/PCOS phenotype; holistic metabolic strategy with cycle control.
  • Female amenorrhoea after weight loss + low/normal LH/FSH ‚Üí functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea; nutrition/restoration before hormones.

Decision Guard‚ÄëRails

  • Confirm abnormalities twice (same lab, proper timing) before diagnosing chronic deficiency.
  • Don‚Äôt treat a number‚Äîtreat a person with symptoms, risks, and goals.
  • Think bones, mood, metabolic health, and fertility at each review.
