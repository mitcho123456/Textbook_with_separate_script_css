Reading the Full Blood Count

Introduction: The Art of Reading Numbers

Every FBC tells a story. The skill lies not in memorising normal ranges, but in recognising patterns, understanding relationships between parameters, and translating numerical data into clinical meaning. This chapter teaches you to read FBC results like a detective examining clues, where each parameter provides evidence about what's happening inside your patient's bone marrow and circulation.

Clinical Pearl

"Never Read in Isolation": A single abnormal value means little - it's the pattern of abnormalities that reveals the diagnosis. Hb 95 g/L with MCV 65 fl tells a completely different story than Hb 95 g/L with MCV 110 fl, even though both represent anaemia.

Step 1: The Systematic Approach to Reading FBC

First Glance: The Overview

Scan quickly - are all three cell lines (red, white, platelets) normal, or are one, two, or all three abnormal? This immediate pattern recognition guides your entire approach.

Red Cell Analysis: Size Matters

If Hb is low, immediately look at MCV. <80 fl = microcytic (think iron deficiency), >100 fl = macrocytic (think B12/folate), 80-100 fl = normocytic (think chronic disease or bleeding).

White Cell Differential: The Detail Story

Don't just look at total WCC - examine the differential. High neutrophils suggest infection, high lymphocytes may indicate viral infection or lymphoproliferative disorder.

Additional Clues: RDW and Blood Film

High RDW indicates variation in cell sizes - valuable in mixed deficiencies. Always request blood film comments when results don't fit the clinical picture.

Clinical Pearl

"The MCV is Your Best Friend": In anaemia, MCV classification is 90% of your diagnosis. Microcytic = iron deficiency until proven otherwise. Macrocytic = B12/folate deficiency until proven otherwise. This simple rule will guide you correctly in the vast majority of cases.

Step 2: Decoding Anaemia Patterns

Microcytic Anaemia (MCV <80 fl)

Classic Pattern: Hb ‚Üì, MCV ‚Üì, RDW ‚Üë
Think: Iron deficiency (95% of cases)
Next Steps: Check ferritin, consider thalassaemia trait if ferritin normal
Red Flag: In men >50 or post-menopausal women, always investigate source of blood loss

Macrocytic Anaemia (MCV >100 fl)

Classic Pattern: Hb ‚Üì, MCV ‚Üë, often with neutrophil hypersegmentation
Think: B12 or folate deficiency
Next Steps: Check B12 and folate levels
Red Flag: Neurological symptoms suggest B12 deficiency requiring urgent treatment

Normocytic Anaemia (MCV 80-100 fl)

Classic Pattern: Hb ‚Üì, MCV normal, variable RDW
Think: Chronic disease, acute blood loss, or mixed deficiency
Next Steps: Check inflammatory markers, haematinics
Red Flag: Sudden onset suggests acute bleeding

Clinical Pearl

"The RDW Reveals Mixed Stories": Normal MCV with raised RDW (>15%) suggests mixed deficiency - some cells are small (iron deficient) and some large (B12/folate deficient), averaging out to normal. Always check all haematinics when RDW is elevated.

üìà

Summary: Mastering FBC Interpretation

The Five-Step FBC Reading Method

  1. Overview Scan: Normal, one abnormal, or multiple abnormalities?
  2. MCV Classification: If anaemic, let MCV guide your thinking
  3. Pattern Recognition: Look for classic combinations
  4. Clinical Correlation: Does the result fit the patient?
  5. Action Planning: Investigation, treatment, or referral needed?

Clinical Pearl

"Practice Makes Pattern Recognition Perfect": The more FBCs you interpret systematically, the faster you'll recognise patterns. Start with MCV in anaemia, examine all three cell lines for abnormalities, and always correlate with clinical picture. These skills will serve you throughout your clinical career.

Introduction: The Deficiency Detective Work

Iron deficiency, B12 deficiency, and folate deficiency are among the most common and treatable causes of anaemia worldwide. Yet diagnosis can be complex, with overlapping presentations and confounding factors. Modern testing allows precise diagnosis, but requires systematic interpretation.

Clinical Pearl

"Ferritin is Your Friend, But Not Always Honest":‚Å£ Ferritin <15 ¬µg/L always means iron deficiency. Ferritin >100 ¬µg/L usually excludes iron deficiency. But ferritin 15-100 ¬µg/L in the presence of inflammation can still represent iron deficiency.

Iron Studies - Systematic Interpretation

Iron Deficiency Pattern

Classic: Low ferritin (<15), Low iron, High TIBC, Low transferrin saturation
Action: Treat with iron, investigate source of loss
Follow-up: Check response at 8 weeks

Iron Deficiency + Inflammation

Tricky: Ferritin 15-100, Low transferrin saturation <20%
Key: Transferrin saturation is most reliable
Action: Trial of iron therapy

üß™

B12 and Folate Assessment

Clinical Pearl

"B12 Deficiency Can Hide":‚Å£ Normal B12 levels don't exclude functional deficiency. If clinical suspicion is high with neurological symptoms, consider treatment trial even with borderline results.

When to Suspect B12/Folate Deficiency

  • Macrocytic anaemia (MCV >100 fl)
  • Neurological symptoms (B12 especially)
  • Glossitis, angular stomatitis
  • Poor dietary intake, malabsorption
  • Certain medications (metformin, PPIs)

Introduction: Reading the Liver's Language

Liver function tests are among the most commonly ordered investigations in primary care, yet interpretation can be challenging. The key is understanding that different patterns tell different stories - hepatocellular injury looks different from cholestasis, acute problems differ from chronic disease.

Clinical Pearl

"Pattern Over Numbers": ALT 300 with normal ALP suggests hepatocellular injury. ALP 300 with normal ALT suggests cholestasis. The pattern matters more than absolute values.

Essential Liver Function Test Ranges

Test Normal Range Units Key Points
ALT 10-40 U/L More liver-specific than AST
AST 10-35 U/L Also found in heart, muscle
ALP 40-130 U/L Bone and liver isoforms
Bilirubin 3-20 ¬µmol/L Conjugated vs unconjugated
Albumin 35-50 g/L Reflects synthetic function
üéØ

Step-by-Step LFT Interpretation

Step 1: Identify the Pattern

Hepatocellular (ALT/AST high, ALP normal) vs Cholestatic (ALP high, ALT/AST normal/mild) vs Mixed pattern

Step 2: Assess Severity

Mild (<2x normal), Moderate (2-5x), Severe (>5x normal). Very high ALT (>1000) suggests acute hepatocellular necrosis

Step 3: Look at Synthetic Function

Albumin and PT/INR reflect liver's synthetic capacity. Low albumin with raised liver enzymes is concerning

üß©

Common LFT Patterns and Their Meaning

Acute Hepatitis Pattern

Pattern: Very high ALT/AST (>500), Normal/mildly raised ALP
Think: Viral hepatitis, paracetamol toxicity, ischaemic hepatitis
Action: Urgent assessment, hepatitis screen, medication review

Cholestatic Pattern

Pattern: High ALP (>2x normal), Normal/mildly raised ALT
Think: Gallstones, drug-induced, primary biliary cholangitis
Action: USS abdomen, medication review, consider MRCP

Chronic Liver Disease

Pattern: Mildly abnormal enzymes, Low albumin, Prolonged PT
Think: Cirrhosis, chronic hepatitis
Action: Alcohol history, hepatitis screen, consider referral

Clinical Pearl

"The AST:ALT Ratio Rule": AST:ALT ratio >2 in the context of liver disease strongly suggests alcohol-related liver disease. This simple ratio can be more diagnostic than absolute values.

Introduction: The Kidney's Report Card

Urea and electrolytes testing provides a window into kidney function, fluid balance, and electrolyte homeostasis. Modern eGFR calculations have revolutionised how we assess kidney function, but understanding the full U&E picture requires systematic interpretation of multiple parameters.

Clinical Pearl

"eGFR Tells You Where, Creatinine Tells You How Fast": eGFR gives you the chronic picture - what stage of CKD. Rising creatinine tells you about acute changes. Both matter for different reasons.

AKI Stage 1
Creatinine rise >26 ¬µmol/L in 48h or >1.5x baseline

Clinical Pearl

"The Urea:Creatinine Ratio Reveals the Story": Normal ratio ~10:1. High ratio (>20:1) suggests pre-renal cause (dehydration, heart failure). Normal ratio with raised creatinine suggests intrinsic renal disease.

Electrolyte Emergencies

Dangerous Electrolyte Levels

  • Sodium <125 mmol/L: Confusion, seizures possible
  • Sodium >155 mmol/L: Severe dehydration, brain damage risk
  • Potassium <2.5 mmol/L: Cardiac arrhythmias, paralysis
  • Potassium >6.0 mmol/L: Cardiac arrest risk - ECG immediately
