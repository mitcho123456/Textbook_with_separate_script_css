Introduction: The Body's Chemical Balance Sheet

Urea and electrolytes aren't just numbers on a lab reportâ€”they're a window into your patient's fluid balance, kidney function, and metabolic state. Every result tells part of a larger story: creatinine whispers about kidney health, sodium shouts about fluid status, potassium warns about cardiac risk, and urea reveals the balance between production and clearance. Understanding these relationships transforms you from someone who flags abnormal values into a clinician who can predict problems before they become critical.

This chapter teaches you to read U&Es like a detective examining clues. You'll learn to spot acute kidney injury before it becomes irreversible, recognize dangerous electrolyte imbalances before they cause arrhythmias, and understand when mild abnormalities signal major underlying pathology. Most importantly, you'll know when to act immediately and when to observe carefully.

Clinical Pearl

"The eGFR Revolution": Don't just look at creatinineâ€”eGFR is what matters. A creatinine of 120 Î¼mol/L might be normal for a 25-year-old muscular man (eGFR >90) but represents severe kidney disease in a 75-year-old woman (eGFR <30). Always interpret creatinine in the context of age, sex, and muscle mass via eGFR calculation.

However, certain patterns represent immediate threats to lifeâ€”particularly cardiac arrhythmias from potassium imbalance or cerebral edema from sodium disorders. Step 1: Kidney Function Assessment - Beyond Creatinine

Kidney function assessment has evolved far beyond simply checking if creatinine is elevated. The combination of eGFR, creatinine trends, and urea patterns tells you not just whether the kidneys are failing, but how quickly, why, and what you can do about it. Understanding these relationships is crucial for early detection and intervention.

Always Check eGFR First

eGFR adjusts creatinine for age, sex, and ethnicity, giving true kidney function. Use CKD-EPI equation (standard in UK labs). eGFR >60 = normal kidney function, 30-60 = moderate CKD, <30 = severe CKD requiring specialist care.

Assess Chronicity vs Acuity

Compare with previous results. Rising creatinine over months = chronic kidney disease. Doubling in days = acute kidney injury. Mixed pattern suggests acute-on-chronic deterioration needing urgent assessment.

Examine the Urea:Creatinine Ratio

Normal ratio ~10:1. High ratio (>20:1) suggests prerenal AKI, dehydration, or GI bleeding. Low ratio suggests liver disease or low protein intake. This ratio helps identify the cause of kidney dysfunction.

Clinical Context Integration

Combine biochemistry with symptoms (oliguria, edema), examination (fluid status, blood pressure), and drug history (ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs, diuretics). Numbers alone don't make diagnoses.

Step 2: Sodium Disorders - The Osmotic Balancing Act

Hyponatremia (<135 mmol/L)

Mild (130-135): Often asymptomatic, investigate cause
Moderate (125-129): Nausea, confusion, falls risk
Severe (<125): Seizures, coma, cerebral edema
Key: Assess volume status and urine osmolality

Hypernatremia (>145 mmol/L)

Mild (146-150): Thirst, restlessness
Moderate (151-160): Confusion, muscle twitching
Severe (>160): Seizures, coma, cerebral hemorrhage
Cause: Usually water loss > sodium loss

SIADH Pattern

Biochemistry: Low Na+, low serum osmolality, high urine osmolality
Clinical: Euvolemic, no edema
Causes: CNS disease, lung disease, drugs, malignancy
Treatment: Fluid restriction, treat underlying cause

Dehydration Pattern

Biochemistry: High Na+, high serum osmolality
Clinical: Dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor
Causes: Inadequate fluid intake, diabetes insipidus
Treatment: Gradual rehydration with hypotonic fluids

Hyponatremia Causes

Hypovolemic: Diuretics, diarrhea, vomiting, burns

Euvolemic: SIADH, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency

Hypervolemic: Heart failure, liver failure, kidney failure

SIADH Common Causes

CNS: Stroke, head injury, meningitis, tumors

Pulmonary: Pneumonia, lung cancer, TB

Drugs: SSRIs, carbamazepine, thiazides, PPIs

Hypernatremia Causes

Water loss: Diabetes insipidus, diarrhea, sweating

Sodium excess: IV hypertonic saline, salt poisoning

Impaired thirst: Elderly, dementia, unconscious patients

Critical Correction Rules

Chronic hyponatremia: Correct <8 mmol/L per 24h

Acute severe: Can correct faster but watch for CPM

Hypernatremia: Correct <10 mmol/L per 24h

Clinical Pearl

"The Central Pontine Myelinolysis Fear": Correcting chronic hyponatremia too rapidly (>8 mmol/L per 24 hours) can cause irreversible brainstem damage. If sodium is chronically low (>48 hours), go slow. Use 3% saline only for acute severe hyponatremia with neurological symptoms, and monitor hourly. When in doubt, correct conservatively.

Step 3: Potassium - The Cardiac Critical Parameter

Potassium disorders are among the most immediately life-threatening electrolyte abnormalities. Both hyperkalemia and hypokalemia can cause fatal arrhythmias, but the clinical presentation can be deceptively mild until sudden cardiovascular collapse. Understanding the causes, recognizing the ECG changes, and knowing the urgent treatment protocols is essential for every clinician.

Normal Potassium (3.5-5.0 mmol/L)

Physiology: 98% intracellular, crucial for membrane potential
ECG: Normal P-waves, QRS, and T-waves
Clinical: Normal muscle and cardiac function

Hypokalemia (<3.5 mmol/L)

Mild (3.0-3.4): Often asymptomatic, muscle weakness
Severe (<3.0): Paralysis, rhabdomyolysis risk
ECG: U-waves, T-wave flattening, ST depression
Risk: Digoxin toxicity, arrhythmias

Hyperkalemia (>5.0 mmol/L)

Mild (5.1-5.9): Usually asymptomatic
Moderate (6.0-6.4): ECG changes likely
Severe (>6.5): Life-threatening arrhythmia risk
ECG: Tall T-waves â†’ wide QRS â†’ sine wave

Critical Hyperkalemia (>6.5 mmol/L)

Immediate action: Calcium gluconate 10mL 10%
Shift K+ intracellular: Insulin + glucose, salbutamol
Remove K+: Calcium resonium, dialysis
Monitor: Continuous ECG, hourly K+ levels

Hyperkalemia Emergency Management Protocol

Immediate Assessment (K+ >6.0 mmol/L)

12-lead ECG immediately. Look for tall T-waves, wide QRS, absent P-waves. Any ECG changes or K+ >6.5 mmol/L = medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.

Cardiac Protection (First Priority)

Calcium gluconate 10mL 10% IV over 2-5 minutes. Protects heart from arrhythmias within minutes. Repeat if ECG changes persist. Effect lasts 30-60 minutes.

Shift Potassium Intracellular

Insulin 10 units + glucose 50mL 50% IV. Alternative: Salbutamol 10-20mg nebulized. Both shift K+ into cells within 15-30 minutes. Monitor glucose closely.

Remove Potassium from Body

Calcium resonium 15g PO/PR if GI tract working. Consider urgent dialysis if K+ >7.0 mmol/L or not responding to medical treatment. Stop all K+-sparing drugs.

Hyperkalemia Causes

Decreased excretion: AKI, CKD, ACE inhibitors, spironolactone
Increased intake: K+ supplements, salt substitutes
Cell shift: Acidosis, tissue breakdown, succinylcholine
Pseudohyperkalemia: Hemolysis, thrombocytosis, delayed processing

Hypokalemia Causes

GI losses: Diarrhea, vomiting, fistulas
Renal losses: Diuretics, hyperaldosteronism, RTA
Cell shift: Insulin, Î²2-agonists, alkalosis
Poor intake: Anorexia, alcoholism, elderly

Drug-Induced Hyperkalemia

Common drugs: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, spironolactone, amiloride
Risk factors: CKD, elderly, dehydration
Management: Stop offending drug, monitor closely
Prevention: Regular monitoring in high-risk patients

Replacement Therapy

Mild hypokalemia: Oral K+ 40-80 mmol daily
Severe hypokalemia: IV K+ max 10 mmol/hour
Monitor: ECG, repeat K+ after each 40 mmol
Mg+ check: Always correct hypomagnesemia first

Clinical Pearl

"The Magnesium Connection": Hypokalemia that doesn't correct despite adequate potassium replacement suggests concurrent hypomagnesemia. Magnesium is essential for potassium retention. Always check and correct magnesium levels when treating persistent hypokalemiaâ€”you can't fix low potassium without fixing low magnesium first.

Step 4: Acid-Base Balance - Reading Between the Lines

While U&Es don't give you complete acid-base status, the bicarbonate level provides crucial information about metabolic acidosis and alkalosis. Combined with the anion gap (if available) and clinical context, bicarbonate changes can reveal serious underlying pathology from diabetic ketoacidosis to kidney failure.

Understanding Bicarbonate Changes

Bicarbonate (HCO3-) reflects the metabolic component of acid-base balance:

  • Low HCO3- (<22): Metabolic acidosis
  • High HCO3- (>29): Metabolic alkalosis
  • Normal (22-29): No metabolic acid-base disorder

Remember: the body compensates, so chronic respiratory disorders cause predictable metabolic changes.

Clinical Approach to Bicarbonate Abnormalities

Always consider the clinical context:

  • Acidosis + diabetes: Check ketones, glucose
  • Acidosis + kidney disease: Uremic acidosis
  • Alkalosis + hypokalemia: Diuretics, vomiting
  • Severe acidosis: Consider toxic ingestion

Metabolic Acidosis (HCO3- <22)

Mild (18-21): Often compensated, investigate cause
Moderate (12-17): Symptoms likely, treat underlying cause
Severe (<12): Life-threatening, consider bicarbonate
Causes: DKA, lactic acidosis, kidney failure, diarrhea

Metabolic Alkalosis (HCO3- >29)

Mild (30-35): Usually well tolerated
Severe (>40): Respiratory depression risk
Causes: Vomiting, diuretics, hyperaldosteronism
Treatment: Correct K+ and Cl- deficiency

Diabetic Ketoacidosis Pattern

HCO3-: Usually <15 mmol/L
Associated: High glucose, ketones, anion gap
Clinical: Dehydration, Kussmaul breathing
Treatment: Insulin, fluids, K+ replacement

Chronic Compensation

COPD: High HCO3- compensates for high CO2
Normal in COPD: HCO3- may be 30-35 mmol/L
Key point: Don't "correct" compensated states
Danger: Rapid changes in chronic patients

Clinical Pearl

"The Anion Gap Detective": When bicarbonate is low, calculate the anion gap: (Na+ + K+) - (Cl- + HCO3-). Normal is 8-16. High anion gap acidosis (DKA, lactic acidosis, toxins) needs urgent treatment. Normal anion gap acidosis (diarrhea, RTA) is usually less urgent but still needs investigation.

Step 5: Putting It All Together - Pattern Recognition

Start with Kidney Function

Check eGFR first. Is this normal kidney function, CKD, or AKI? This sets the context for interpreting all other electrolyte abnormalities and guides urgency of intervention.

Assess Life-Threatening Electrolytes

Check K+ for arrhythmia risk, Na+ for seizure risk. These need immediate attention if critically abnormal, regardless of other findings.

Look for Patterns

Multiple abnormalities suggest systemic disease. AKI + hyperkalemia = medication review. Low Na+ + high K+ = adrenal insufficiency. Pattern recognition speeds diagnosis.

Clinical Integration

Combine biochemistry with clinical picture. Dehydration, heart failure, medication history all influence interpretation and management priorities.

Dehydration Pattern

Biochemistry: High urea:creatinine ratio, high sodium
Clinical: Dry mucous membranes, reduced urine output
Causes: Poor fluid intake, diabetes, diuretics
Treatment: Careful fluid replacement, monitor response

Heart Failure Pattern

Biochemistry: Mild AKI, low sodium, high urea
Clinical: Edema, elevated JVP, breathlessness
Cause: Poor cardiac output, activation of RAAS
Treatment: Optimize heart failure management

Addison's Disease Pattern

Biochemistry: Low Na+, high K+, mild AKI
Clinical: Hypotension, pigmentation, weakness
Test: 9am cortisol, short synacthen test
Treatment: Urgent steroids if suspected

Diuretic Effect Pattern

Thiazides: Low K+, low Na+, mild AKI
Loop diuretics: Low K+, dehydration
K+-sparing: High K+, especially with ACE inhibitors
Management: Adjust doses, monitor regularly

AKI Risk Factors

Age >65, CKD, heart failure, liver disease, diabetes

Dehydration, sepsis, contrast exposure

NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, gentamicin

Hyperkalemia Risk Factors

CKD, diabetes, heart failure

ACE inhibitors, ARBs, spironolactone

Age >65, acidosis, tissue breakdown

Hyponatremia Risk Factors

Age >65, female sex, low BMI

Diuretics, SSRIs, carbamazepine

Heart failure, liver disease, COPD

Monitoring Requirements

High-risk patients need regular U&Es

After starting/changing nephrotoxic drugs

During acute illness, dehydration

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

The Creatinine Blind Spot

Mistake: Assuming normal creatinine = normal kidney function
Reality: Elderly, small women can have significant CKD with "normal" creatinine
Solution: Always check eGFR, not just creatinine
Example: 75-year-old woman with creatinine 90 may have eGFR <45

The Pseudohyperkalemia Trap

Mistake: Treating laboratory artifact as true hyperkalemia
Reality: Hemolysis, thrombocytosis, delayed processing cause false high K+
Solution: Check for hemolysis, repeat sample if suspicious
Clues: Normal ECG with very high K+, hemolyzed sample

The Bicarbonate Overreaction

Mistake: Aggressive treatment of compensated acid-base disorders
Reality: COPD patients normally have high bicarbonate
Solution: Don't "correct" chronic compensation
Key: Rapid changes in chronic patients are dangerous

The ACE Inhibitor Knee-Jerk

Mistake: Stopping ACE inhibitors for mild creatinine rise
Reality: 30% creatinine rise is acceptable and protective
Solution: Only stop if >50% rise or hyperkalemia >5.5
Benefit: Long-term kidney and heart protection usually outweighs risk

Red Flags That Should Never Be Missed

  • K+ >6.0 mmol/L: Immediate ECG and treatment regardless of symptoms
  • Na+ <125 mmol/L with confusion: Risk of seizures, assess chronicity urgently
  • Creatinine doubled from baseline: AKI until proven otherwise
  • eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73mÂ²: Pre-dialysis CKD needing urgent nephrology referral
  • HCO3- <12 mmol/L: Life-threatening acidosis, check pH if possible
  • New AKI + rash + eosinophilia: Drug-induced interstitial nephritis

Summary: The Five-Step U&E Mastery Method

1. Check eGFR First

True kidney function assessment, not just creatinine

2. Assess Life-Threatening Values

Potassium for arrhythmias, sodium for seizures

3. Look for Patterns

Multiple abnormalities suggest systemic disease

4. Review Medications

Many U&E problems are drug-related and reversible

5. Monitor Trends

Changes over time more important than single values

Critical Values to Act On Immediately

K+ >6.5 or <2.5: Cardiac protection, immediate treatment

Na+ <125 with symptoms: Assess chronicity, cautious correction

Creatinine doubled: AKI workup, stop nephrotoxic drugs

HCO3- <15: Severe acidosis, investigate cause urgently

Key Clinical Decision Points

eGFR 30-60: Stage 3 CKD, nephrology referral if progressing

eGFR <30: Stage 4-5 CKD, urgent nephrology referral

AKI Stage 3: Consider dialysis, intensive monitoring

Drug-induced changes: Often reversible with medication adjustment

Clinical Pearl

"The Trend is Your Friend": Single abnormal U&E values can be misleading, but trends over time reveal the true story. Slowly rising creatinine over months suggests progressive CKD requiring different management than rapidly rising creatinine over days suggesting AKI. Always interpret results in the context of previous values and rate of change.

Take Home Messages for U&E Interpretation

  • eGFR trumps creatinine: Always assess kidney function via eGFR, especially in elderly patients
  • Potassium kills quickly: Any K+ >6.0 mmol/L needs immediate treatment regardless of symptoms
  • Medication review essential: Many U&E abnormalities are drug-related and reversible
  • AKI is often preventable: Early recognition and intervention can prevent progression
  • Context matters: Combine biochemistry with clinical assessment and drug history
  • Chronic compensation is normal: Don't "fix" appropriately compensated acid-base disorders
